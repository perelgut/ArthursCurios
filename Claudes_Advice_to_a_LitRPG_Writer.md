# Claude's Advice to a Wannabe LitRPG Writer

*A running collection. Append new entries as they land. Each item: 1–3 sentences, no more.*

---

## Pre-prose work

**1. Write the system spec before the prose.** Treat your magic, leveling, and object-sentience rules as a software requirements document — numbers, formulas, edge cases, and what can never happen. LitRPG readers catch rule breaks the way programmers catch missing semicolons; retconning your XP curve three books in is a misery worth avoiding.

**2. Set series-arc checkpoints with explicit power bands.** Write a one-paragraph synopsis for each planned book and note the protagonist's level or power at the start and end of each. This is your guardrail against the genre's most reliable failure mode — book 4 collapsing into god-mode tedium because power crept too fast in books 1–2.

**3. Beat-sheet book 1 at the scene or chapter level.** Pick any framework (Save the Cat, Snowflake, Hero's Journey, Story Grid) and actually use it. Add LitRPG-specific beats: tutorial sequence, first meaningful level-up moment, first system surprise, first death-stakes encounter.

**4. Write a voice guide for each sentient object before drafting.** Half a page each: vocabulary, sentence length, rhythm, values, what they refuse to discuss, origin of their sentience, what they think of the protagonist. The pass-the-test moment is when you can cover the speaker's name in a dialogue exchange and still tell who's talking.

---

## Drafting

**5. Spend the first 5,000–10,000 words deliberately locking in your defaults.** POV, tense, status-window style (Markdown boxes vs. sparse italic notifications vs. in-character interpretation), how thickly to layer system information. Once those decisions are baked in, the rest of book 1 drafts much faster because you're no longer re-deciding them every chapter.

**6. Death and consequence need real weight even when the system technically allows respawn or rollback.** Mechanically permissible is not the same as emotionally costless. The LitRPG series that work tend to use the system as a constraint on character decisions, not a shield from them.

## Tools

**7. Sudowrite's Story Bible is your foundation — but it's not built for tabular rules data.** Keep the system spec, stat tables, and series bible in something structured (Notion, Obsidian, World Anvil, or just Markdown + a spreadsheet) and feed *summaries* of the relevant pieces into Sudowrite's Story Bible per scene.

**8. Use Sudowrite for prose continuation, sensory description, dialogue variants, and exploring two or three takes on a difficult scene.** Don't use it for system-consistency checks, math, or series-arc planning. [Inference, based on observed LLM patterns] An LLM's instinct is to make the story feel cool *now* at the cost of constraints you established earlier — that's a writer-judgment problem, not a tool problem.

**9. Learn Sudowrite project-based against your own material, not generic exercises.** Pour your real notes into the Story Bible and use one feature at a time on your actual opening. You'll learn the tool's voice and failure modes faster because you have strong opinions about your own work.

**10. Start with the cheapest Sudowrite plan or trial credits before committing.** [Inference, my knowledge bounded May 2025] The tool's value depends heavily on whether its prose style works with yours, and that's a per-writer thing — confirm fit before locking into an annual plan.
