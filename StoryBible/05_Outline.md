## Chapter 1: The Key to Arthur's Curios
Stepan Novacek, a 68-year-old retired man with a bad hip and a reliance on a thick ash walking cane, boards an aging train out of Toronto bound for Ingersoll, Ontario. He carries two heavy suitcases, a certified letter from an estate lawyer, and a large, ornate, dark iron key — both the letter and the key connected to his Uncle Arthur, a great-uncle recently declared legally dead after seven years of unexplained absence. Arthur, who had been largely absent from the family for nearly forty years, has left Stepan his curiosity shop in the tiny rural hamlet of Grovesend. Stepan, often meticulous by nature and averse to improvisation, has pre-arranged his transport from Ingersoll — but the grizzled male driver he anticipated turns out to be Danica, known as Dan, a capable and efficient local livery driver who loads both of his heavy suitcases into her Ford F-150 before he can so much as protest. The two set off east toward Grovesend, and during the drive, Danica begins to fill Stepan in on the local reputation of Arthur's Curiosities. The shop has been abandoned since Arthur's disappearance and has accumulated a genuinely unsettling folklore: teenagers refuse to go near it after dark, insisting that things move inside when no one is there; a boy who threw a rock at the building claims it stopped dead in mid-air before dropping onto the porch and cracking his toe; and a town building inspector's truck died every single time he pulled up to the property, only restarting after being towed past the town line. Locals have concluded that the place has a mind of its own and prefers its privacy. Stepan responds to each account with dry, self-deprecating humour, though he listens carefully and misses nothing — including the brief, telling moment when Danica's foot eases off the accelerator upon learning he is Arthur's nephew. As the truck crests a hill, Grovesend appears below: a handful of rooftops nestled beneath ancient trees in a quiet valley, built around a crossroads with a church spire, giving the impression of a place that was forgotten by accident, later rediscovered, and politely asked not to make a fuss. At the edge of that crossroads sits Arthur's Curiosities — dark, narrow, weathered, and leaning beneath a sagging sign, its door painted black and its windows filmed with grime. The chapter closes on an ominous and quietly magical note as the large iron key in Stepan's coat pocket grows warm against his chest the moment his eyes find the building for the first time.
