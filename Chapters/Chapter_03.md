Inventory of Impossible Things
==============================

Chapter Three: Call it Static
-----------------------------

A list, Stepan has always believed, is a way of putting the world on notice. It says: *I have seen you, I have written you down, and I will deal with you in an orderly fashion.* It is the closest thing he has to a religion, and like most religions it works best when you decline to examine the parts that don't.

He sits on the edge of the bed with the phone in his hand and reads the ten items again. Phone, clothes, dishes, food, bread, plumbing, the unpaid-yet-functioning utilities, the sparking cane, the self-unpacking luggage, the key that runs hot and cold. Ten impossible things before breakfast – except breakfast was an hour ago, and several of the impossible things had been *in* it.

A practical man, handed ten problems, sorts them. Some you cannot touch: you cannot hammer a phone battery into a confession or take a jar of marmalade to small-claims court. But two of the ten are wood and nails – the lifted board in the hall that keeps ambushing his toe, and the loose tread on the stairs. Wood and nails he has understood his entire life. Wood and nails stay where you put them and do not, under any circumstances, talk back.

There is, of course, an eleventh item. He did not write it down. He is not going to write it down. He is going to find a hammer instead, because a man with a hammer in his hand is a man with an explanation in his pocket, and Stepan would like, very much, to have an explanation in his pocket.

The cat watches him from the windowsill, tail curled over its feet, with the patient attention of something that has all day and intends to spend the whole of it judging.

\* \* \*

The hammer is on the hall table.

He is fairly sure the hall table was empty yesterday, and he is entirely sure he did not put a hammer on it, but he has decided – as a matter of policy, adopted this morning – to stop being sure of things in this house. The hammer is good and heavy and fits his hand as though it remembers it. He elects not to pursue that thought either.

He lowers himself to one knee, a negotiation conducted in stages with The Complaint Department's overnight skeleton crew, and sets the lifted board down where it belongs and taps the nail home. Two clean strikes and a third for spite. The board sits flush. A small warmth turns over behind his breastbone – the same coal-in-a-banked-fire flicker as yesterday – and he ignores it the way he ignores most things that cannot be itemized.

*That is not how one holds a hammer.*

"It's exactly how you hold a hammer," he says. "You choke up to start the nail and drop your hand for the drive, it gives you the –"

He stops.

His own voice has run a half-second past the point where it had any business answering, because nobody asked. The hall is empty but for him and the cat, and the cat is sitting four feet away with its ears swung full forward and an expression Stepan has never seen on an animal: the precise, arrested stillness of someone who has spoken aloud in a room they were certain was empty.

For a long moment the two of them simply look at each other.

"...Talking to myself," Stepan announces – to the hall, to the cat, to the general management. "Everyone does it. Sign of a fine mind." He levers himself upright, in stages, and does not notice that the cat fails to relax, fails to groom, fails to perform any of the thousand idle things a cat does when nothing is happening. It holds very still. It watches him. And somewhere behind those ridiculous amber eyes, a number it has been keeping for seven years is being, very quietly, revised.

\* \* \*

The stairs are next, and the stairs fight back.

He re-sticks the curled non-slip strip first – grippy rubber peeling at one corner, pressed flat and tacked down, easy. Then the loose tread, fourth from the bottom, the one that shifts sideways underfoot like a handshake from a man you wouldn't lend money to. He gets a few nails into it and it firms up – and that is when his thumb finds the give at the riser's edge: the soft sponge of it, the damp crumble that means rot has been working from underneath for longer than he's been alive. He presses, and his thumb sinks in like it's testing bread.

"Wonderful," he says. A nail and a prayer will hold the top of the tread, but the wood beneath it is going, and replacing a stair tread properly – cutting, fitting, matching the run – is a younger man's job, or at the very least a more skilled one's. He files it, reluctantly, under *Cannot Do Alone.* The list, it seems, has spawned a sub-list. This is how lists breed.

Behind him, descending with the boneless confidence of the entitled, the cat threads itself between his ankles – on a staircase – which is either an act of trust or an attempt on his life.

"Off – *off* – you'll have us both down the –" He catches the rail, and on pure reflex, the reflex he has been so carefully not examining, he points the cane.

*Tick.* A thin blue snap leaps off the brass ferrule and catches the cat on the haunch.

It does not bolt this time. It rounds on him from two steps down, fur up, and the voice arrives in the dead center of his skull, crisp as a struck bell and dripping with an aristocratic affront no cat has ever owned:

*Was that strictly necessary?*

Stepan stands on his own stairs, holding a cane and a cat's withering review of his manners, and feels the cool drop run down the inside of his arm and out through his knuckles – the spent feeling, the something-leaving – and reaches, with both hands, for the railing of the only theory he can still stand on.

"Static," he says. "Dry house. Wool socks. Static." He says it the way other men say grace.

The cat declines to dignify this. It descends the remaining stairs with the bearing of a diplomat departing a country it fully intends to file a report about, and Stepan is left to notice, and instantly un-notice, that he has just answered a question no mouth had asked.

\* \* \*

It is somewhere near the bottom of the stairs that the bill comes due.

He has not done a morning's honest work with his hands in – he does the arithmetic and dislikes the answer – a decade. His shirt is glued to his back. His hands have developed a fine tremor, the kind that turns a signature into a seismograph. And The Complaint Department, which had kept such uncharacteristically gentle hours these past two days, throws open the office, switches on every light, and commences its outreach in earnest: a deep grinding ache in the hip, radiating to the knee, with carbon copies to the lower back.

He stops. He has to. He lowers himself onto the second stair – the sound one – and reaches, without thinking, for his coffee.

The coffee is there. At his elbow. The goblin king grins up at him, the mug half full and impossibly hot, from a counter he is quite certain he left it on at the far end of the shop, one floor and forty feet of clutter away.

He looks at it for a while.

He considers adding it to the list. *Item eleven: the coffee follows me around.* Then he considers how that would look in his own handwriting, and decides instead that he is tired, that a tired man misremembers where he sets things, and that this is an ordinary mug of coffee he ordinarily carried and ordinarily forgot. He drinks it. It is, naturally, perfect.

The cat reappears and arranges itself on the bottom step in a slant of grey window-light, immaculately comfortable, observing him sweat with the frank interest of an audience that did not pay but feels it is getting full value.

"Don't," Stepan tells it.

It doesn't. It just watches. Somehow that is worse.

\* \* \*

The phone rings, and it is so aggressively, blessedly ordinary a sound that he nearly weeps.

"Novacek."

"It's Danica." A pause with a truck engine idling inside it. "Headed your way later. Figured I'd ask before you starve to death out of pride – need anything from town? Ingersoll's got a real grocery, not just the gas station."

It is a kind thing to do for a newcomer, and Stepan opens his mouth to accept, to reel off the dozen sensible things a man needs when he's just landed in a shuttered shop in the middle of nowhere – and discovers he cannot name one. The fridge is full. The larder is full. The bread is fresh, the butter is in its crock, the marmalade is in its jar, and there is, he realizes with a slow descending cold, nothing on this earth he requires that the building has not already, silently, supplied.

"I'm –" He stops. "It's strange. I don't think I need a thing. The place came stocked."

The pause on the other end is a different shape than the last one.

"Stocked," Danica says. Flat. Careful.

"Fridge, pantry, all of it. Arthur must have had a standing order, or the lawyer arranged –" He can hear himself hammering together the scaffold, and keeps going, because the alternative is to stop and look at what it's holding up.

"Stepan." Her voice has gone very level, the tone you'd use near something you didn't want to spook. "That shop's been shut seven years. Nobody's had a key. Nobody delivers out there – I'd know, I'm who'd drive it. There's no standing order to have."

And there it is, from the outside, in another person's mouth: it isn't him. Not fatigue, not a soft memory, not the marmalade. The impossible thing is solid enough that a sensible woman in a Ford F-150 has gone quiet on a phone line over it.

He changes the subject, because that, too, is a thing practical men do. "There's a stair here gone to rot. Past my skill to replace properly. I don't suppose you know anyone who does that kind of –"

"I do that kind of work," Danica says, recovering, a thread of amusement back in it. "Among other things. I'll have a look when I come by."

"I'd be grateful." And then – because the cat has lifted its head at some private frequency and is now staring at the phone with undisguised investment –"And this is going to sound mad. Could you bring fish? Fresh. Caught, not frozen." He glances at the animal. "I've acquired a tenant with opinions."

"...Fish. For the cat." A beat. "Sure. Why not. It's that kind of day." The line clicks dead.

The cat is regarding him with what, in a person, he would be forced to call *approval,* and Stepan does not at all care for how much he enjoys it.

\* \* \*

He wants a task he can do sitting down, and the only honest mess in the building is upstairs.

Getting there is its own small expedition, conducted a tread at a time on the cane and the rail with a running internal memorandum of grievances. But Arthur's study, when he reaches it, is exactly as he left it days ago and as Arthur, presumably, left it years before that: dust lying like grey felt over everything, the leaning towers of books, the skinned-over cup of tea gone to geology at the desk's edge. The one room the shop, for reasons of its own, declines to keep.

He dusts. Not well, and not much – the hip has filed an injunction against ambition – but he draws a cloth across the desktop and the windowsill, and feels, once more, that faint warm flicker of something noticed and tallied, and ignores it once more, on principle.

The papers are a disappointment, which is its own quiet relief. Bills. Receipts. A ledger of unremarkable purchases. A seed catalogue. A blank postcard from a town he's never heard of. He works the desk drawers, one after another, and turns up: pens, a steel rule, a tin of mints fused into a single archaeological lozenge, paper clips, a magnifying glass. No diary fallen open to the fatal page. No letters tied in ribbon. No ancient sandwich gone to civilization; no fortune in gold. Only the ordinary sediment a life leaves behind – which is, somehow, worse than a mystery, because it means Arthur was a real man who sat in this real chair and is really, finally gone.

The grief takes him from the side, the way it has before, for an uncle he barely knew – a man who once turned his childhood into a country and then never visited it again.

*He took it with milk.*

Stepan goes still.

*The tea. The one you keep glaring at like it owes you money.* The voice is lower now, the snap drained out of it. *He sat where you're sitting, and he talked too. To me. For years.* A pause, precise and cold, and not quite as cold as it would like to be. *He never listened either.*

Stepan's hand stops flat on the edge of the desk. He does not turn around. He is sixty-eight years old, and he has spent two days stacking reasonable theories like sandbags against a rising river, and he can feel, with great clarity, the water coming over the top of them.

Because *that* he did not know. Could not have known. Has never thought, had no earthly avenue by which to think, that his uncle took his tea with milk.

"I'm losing my mind," he tells the dust. And it is, he realizes, the most comforting thing he has said all day. A mind can be lost. A lost mind can be found, treated, explained. A lost mind is, at the very least, the sort of thing that goes on a list.

\* \* \*

He sits down on the edge of the bed for the second time that day – hollowed out, every theory waterlogged – and the cat pads in, springs to the dresser, and looks at him. And Stepan does the thing a rational man swears, on his life, he will never do.

"All right," he says, aloud, to a cat. "If you've got something to say. Say it."

A test. A dare. A door opened with the eyes shut.

*Finally,* says the cat, with the vast and weary satisfaction of royalty whose carriage has at last been brought around. *Have you any idea how long it has been? Seven years of mealtimes and not one soul with the wit to hear a simple request. The food is beneath comment. The company – present management included – is worse.*

"Stop." He presses the heels of both hands into his eyes. "Stop. You're – you're a cat."

*I am a great many things,* it informs him, with enormous dignity. *A cat is the least of them, and at present the loudest.*

"And the whole time, you've been –"

*Talking. Yes. Largely about you. You are an inattentive host, your coffee habit borders on the clinical, and you snore in a key I would not wish upon an enemy of the state.*

It goes on. It has, plainly, a backlog. It holds forth on the temperature of its cream, the indignity of a plastic bowl, the placement of the litter box (provided, like everything else, by the shop, and resented on principle), and the specific premeditated insult of being served breakfast *on the floor.* The complaints arrive in full paragraphs – beautifully built, withering, and utterly, unmistakably *not his own voice,* because Stepan would not, in a hundred years, squander this much vocabulary on a dish of cream.

So he surrenders the only way he knows how: wearily, sarcastically, the way he'd give in to any small tyrant who had simply outlasted him.

"Yes, fine. Whatever you say." He drags a hand down his face. "Yes, Your Highness."

The cat stops.

It is a small thing, and Stepan – mid-eye-roll, three-quarters broken – does not see it: the way the animal goes rigid on the dresser, the way the amber eyes blow wide for half a heartbeat, the way something that is neither quite fear nor quite hope crosses a face that has worn nothing but contempt for seven unbroken years. By the time he lowers his hand, it is gone, folded away, the imperious mask set neatly back in place as though it had never slipped.

*...How droll,* the cat says, a half-beat too late.

It gathers itself. It sits up, wraps its tail about its feet, and delivers, with the brisk finality of a monarch closing an audience:

*Two things. First: thank you for the fish. Fresh-caught, you said – see that it is cooked. I am not an animal.* A pause. *Second, and do attend, because it matters rather more than you presently grasp: I would not mention to anyone that you and the cat have been holding conversations. The others cannot hear me. They will only hear you. And they will draw the obvious, unkind conclusion about a man of your years, alone in a haunted shop, talking to the furniture.*

And with that it drops to the floor and stalks out, tail high, leaving Stepan Novacek on the edge of the bed in a soft grey T-shirt that reads *Call it Magic, Call it Luck* – having called it static for precisely as long as a man can, and finding, at last, that he has nothing left to call it at all.
