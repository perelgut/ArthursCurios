Inventory of Impossible Things
==============================

Chapter Two: Settling In
------------------------

There is an hour, somewhere in the deep of the night, that belongs entirely to men of a certain age and the plumbing that governs them. Stepan keeps the appointment without ever fully waking for it.

He surfaces from sleep the way a log surfaces in a pond --- slowly, and with no opinion on the matter. Some ancient committee in the base of his skull has filed a single, non-negotiable item of business, and his body rises to attend it before his mind has been so much as copied on the memo. He swings his legs off the bed.

His foot comes down on something warm and indignant.

The something relocates with a flounce he is far too asleep to interpret, and his next step lands a hair from a tail he does not know is there, because in sixty-eight years Stepan has never owned a cat and has therefore never learned the small nighttime arithmetic of where a cat will be. He registers, dimly, a presence. He does not pursue the thought. Thoughts, at this hour, are for the wealthy.

He shuffles toward the bathroom. The door, which he is fairly sure he left ajar, is somehow more ajar by the time he reaches it, swinging wide with the quiet courtesy of a thing that would prefer not to be thanked. He does not notice. A man on this errand notices nothing that is not directly load-bearing.

The bathroom performs as bathrooms do. The water runs warm without being asked twice. He washes his hands at a basin that has no business being this clean and dries them on a towel that has no business being this soft, and he holds no business with either. He pads back across the hall, finds the bed exactly where he left it --- a small mercy he does not pause to appreciate --- and is asleep again before the mattress has finished welcoming him.

Behind him, the door drifts gently back to the precise angle he imagines he left it. The cat watches all of this from the foot of the bed with the expression of an auditor who has seen the books and is choosing, for now, to say nothing.

\* \* \*

Morning arrives properly, with light through a cracked window and the smell of a place that has decided to be lived in.

Stepan wakes the way he prefers to do everything: deliberately, and in stages, like a man lowering himself into cold water. He is, this time, fully and conscientiously awake, which is how he comes to notice that the cat is occupying the exact center of the doorway, arranged with the boneless confidence of an animal that has never once doubted its right to a floor.

"Excuse me," he tells it, and --- because he is a careful man, and because he has now formed a working theory that this particular cat is the sort to lodge a complaint --- he steps around it rather than over it. It accepts the courtesy as its due.

He opens the door himself. He is rather proud of this, in the way one is proud of small competencies upon waking in a strange house. He is less proud, a moment later, when his bare toe finds a floorboard that has lifted a stubborn quarter-inch out of true and announces itself directly into his nervous system.

"*Ow* --- all right. Noted." He glares down at the offending plank. "You and I will have a conversation. With a hammer." He makes the kind of mental note that a man of sixty-eight makes with full knowledge that it will not survive the walk back to the bedroom.

It does not. By the time he returns, the note has been quietly overwritten by something more pressing: his suitcases are gone.

Not gone. He locates them, after a beat of pure animal alarm, standing neatly in the closet, lids open, empty. His shirts hang from the rail in a tidy regiment. His trousers are folded over hangers. His socks --- and here something in him pauses, the way a man pauses at the top of a staircase he doesn't remember climbing --- his socks are paired and ranked in the top drawer like recruits at inspection, where ordinarily they exist in a state of civil war.

He stands and considers this.

The considering does not last long, because the alternative to a reasonable explanation is an unreasonable one, and Stepan does not deal in those before coffee. He must have unpacked last night. Half-asleep, on autopilot, in that diligent fugue the body sometimes manages when the mind has clocked out. People did such things. He has read about it. Sleepwalkers who made sandwiches. It is, he decides, exactly the sort of thing he would do --- tidy in his sleep and forget it by morning --- and he files it there, under *me, probably,* and does not examine the fact that he has never in his life ironed a shirt with the crisp, professional malice now pressed into every one of them.

He dresses in jeans and a soft, much-laundered T-shirt, its slogan worn to a friendly grey. *Call it Magic, Call it Luck,* it says, beneath a faded horseshoe. He bought it years ago because it made him snort in a gift shop, and he has worn it ever since because it is comfortable and because, on some mornings, it is the closest thing he has to a philosophy.

\* \* \*

The stairs offer their own small editorial as he descends.

The fourth tread gives under his weight with a soft, sideways shift --- a loose riser, the nails backed out over decades of feet. "Two," he says aloud, appending it to the morning's growing list of repairs, and notes also that the rubber non-slip strip on the lower steps has curled up at one corner like a stale sandwich, a lawsuit waiting for a wetter day. He is a man who reads a building the way other men read a newspaper, for the small bad news in the back pages.

The shop, glimpsed through the doorway at the bottom, is doing its impression of daytime. Light comes grey and underwater through the front windows, which are filmed enough to admit the fact of the sun without committing to any of the details. He is not, by his own cheerful admission, a morning observer. The shop could be hiding a brass band behind the shelving and he would, before coffee, give it the benefit of the doubt.

The kitchen, by contrast, is wide awake. A cast-iron skillet sits on the stovetop, exactly where a man would want it. The refrigerator, when he opens it, presents fresh bacon, a carton of eggs, orange juice, a small bottle of cream, and milk, arranged with the quiet pride of a fridge that has been waiting up.

He decides not to ask the fridge any questions it isn't prepared to answer.

He sets bacon hissing in the skillet, takes down a plate, and retrieves his mug from yesterday --- the cheerful green one, with the crowned skull and the line *My other mug is a goblin king's skull,* which still, on a second day's acquaintance, makes the corner of his mouth twitch. From a low cupboard he produces two hard plastic bowls, fills one with water and one with a generous splash of cream, and sets them by the wall. He is not, he tells himself, a cat person. He is merely a person in proximity to a cat, conducting basic diplomacy.

The cat arrives precisely when the bacon does, as if summoned by a frequency only the entitled can hear. It springs to the table, sits, and regards the strips of bacon with naked, aristocratic longing. Then, registering that bacon is not on offer and that begging is beneath it, it descends to the cream with the air of a monarch agreeing, under protest, to a reduced budget.

"Glad we could come to terms," Stepan says.

He turns to the coffee maker, the one object in the room he has decided not to think too hard about: a smooth dark monolith broken only by a spout and a single button, with nothing to plug in and nowhere to pour. He presses the button. A ribbon of coffee runs out, hot and unhurried, and he takes it black, the way God and decent dentistry intended.

He scrambles eggs with a little cream, and it is while he is plating them that he turns and discovers a toaster on the counter that he is entirely certain was not there a moment ago.

He looks at it. It looks, being a toaster, like a toaster.

"Well," he says. "I clearly need new glasses." He decides he must have walked past it yesterday in his fog and simply failed to log it, which is a generous interpretation of yesterday and an unkind one of his powers of observation, and he accepts both rather than consider a third option. Beside it sits a loaf of sourdough, the good kind, the crust crackling faintly as if it has just been thinking about an oven. He saws off two slices and drops them in. When he opens the fridge again there is a small crock of butter and a jar of orange marmalade, homemade by the look of the handwriting on the lid, that he is willing to swear were not there ninety seconds ago and is not, at this exact moment, willing to swear about.

He plates the lot --- eggs, bacon, toast gleaming with butter and marmalade --- and then, after a pause, fixes a second small plate with a little egg and a single rasher of bacon and sets it on the floor.

The cat is, visibly, *offended.* Not by the food. By the *floor.* It looks at the plate, then at the table, then at Stepan, with an expression that communicates, across the whole gulf between species, that it is accustomed to dining at the head of something and that this arrangement is a temporary indignity it will be raising at the next available meeting.

And then it eats, because fresh eggs cooked in cream and good bacon are good bacon, and even outrage has its price. It eats with its tail up and its ears at a wounded angle, every line of it composed to make clear that it is doing him an enormous favor.

Stepan laughs --- a real one, surprised out of him. "Your Majesty honors the linoleum," he says, and sits down to his own breakfast feeling, for the first time in longer than he can comfortably measure, something uncomplicatedly like content.

\* \* \*

He carries the dishes to the sink and leaves the cat to its cream, and he washes up.

It is a small thing, washing up. He has done it ten thousand times in ten thousand kitchens and never once felt anything but the mild satisfaction of order restored. So he is mildly surprised, sponge in hand, to feel something else: a faint warm flicker, low behind the breastbone, there and gone, like a coal turning over in a banked fire. He pauses. It does not repeat. Indigestion, he concludes --- the marmalade, probably --- and goes on scrubbing.

From the front of the shop comes a sound.

It is not a large sound. A dry scratch, brief and precise, like a fingernail drawn once across paper, or the single stroke of a pen. He stops, hands dripping, and listens. Nothing follows it. He sets the plate down and goes to look, drying his hands on his jeans, and finds the shop floor exactly as dim and silent and overstuffed as it was the night before, every shelf minding its own business, every shadow holding still.

"Hello?" he says, and feels foolish, because the only thing more useless than asking an empty room a question is waiting for the answer.

On his way back the cat materializes underfoot, and he comes within a heartbeat of going down. He catches himself on the doorframe, heart performing an unscheduled solo, and brandishes his cane at the animal on pure reflex.

"*Get out of my way, Annoying Cat.*"

A spark leaps off the brass ferrule.

It is small --- a blue-white snap, no longer than a fingernail, there and gone with a tiny audible *tick* --- and it catches the cat square in the flank. The cat goes from a standing start to the far doorway in a single liquid streak and vanishes, dignity in tatters, tail enormous.

Stepan stares at the cane.

"...Huh." He turns it in his hand, examining the dull old brass as though it might explain itself. "You have never done that before." He looks around the cluttered shop, hunting for the culprit and settling, gratefully, on the obvious one. "Static. Whole place is dry as a cracker. Wool socks and a dry floor and there's your science." A faint tingle runs up his forearm and dissolves, and he assigns it, too, to static, because static is a thing that exists and he has heard of it, and because the other explanations are still standing politely in the hall waiting to be invited in, and he is not ready to open that door.

\* \* \*

When he comes back to the kitchen, it has tidied itself.

The dishes he left dripping in the rack are dried and put away. The skillet is clean and back on its hook. The toaster --- the toaster he definitely, *certainly* used twenty minutes ago --- is gone, returned to wherever toasters go, and the bread is sealed away in a bag he does not remember owning. Only his coffee mug remains, exactly where his hand last set it, as if the kitchen understands that a man does not surrender his coffee mid-cup for anyone.

He stands in the doorway and feels the first genuine prickle of something that is not coffee and is not static.

"I am not," he says slowly, to the kitchen, "*usually* this tidy. And I am certainly not this forgetful." The two halves of the sentence sit there, refusing to be reconciled, because a forgetful man does not also clean, and a tidy man does not forget that he did.

He begins, methodically, to investigate. He opens the cupboards: dishes stacked, glasses ranked, everything dust-free and squared away. He opens the drawers: utensils sorted, a roll of good knives, a whisk, a fish slice, the full and slightly intimidating armory of a kitchen built by someone who took the work seriously. He opens the stove, which is clean. He opens the fridge again, which is full. It is, he concludes, a complete and functioning kitchen of impeccable 1970s pedigree --- copper-bottomed, cast-iron, no plastic where there might be steel --- with a small handful of upgrades that do not match the vintage and do not match anything else either.

He returns, inevitably, to the coffee maker.

He picks up his mug and sips. The coffee is perfect. Not perfect-when-poured and now sliding toward the tepid compromise that coffee always makes with time, but *perfect,* still, twenty minutes on --- as hot and bright as the first mouthful, with no business being so.

"Now *that,*" he says, with real admiration, "is a mug." He turns it in his hands, the goblin king grinning back at him, and concludes that it must be one of those clever insulated jobs, double-walled, vacuum-sealed, the sort of thing a nephew gives you for Christmas and you pretend to understand. The thought pleases him. It is a small, solvable thing in a morning that has offered too few of them.

The monolith he is less able to dismiss, but he gives it a good try. Some sort of battery, he decides --- a large one, a clever one, the kind of capacity they are always promising is just around the corner. There will be a panel somewhere, a retracting plug, a hatch he hasn't found. He tips it forward an inch and looks beneath it and finds, as before, nothing: no cord, no port, no maker's mark, no concession to the laws under which the rest of the kitchen operates. He sets it down.

"You keep your secrets," he tells it. "I'll keep my coffee. We'll get along fine."

\* \* \*

He goes back upstairs, coffee in hand, to begin the day in earnest, and the fourth tread shifts under his foot again. "*Two,*" he says, with feeling, and resolves --- again, sincerely, uselessly --- to find a hammer.

At the top of the stairs, the cat is waiting for him.

It is not lounging this time. It is *positioned* --- planted dead center on the landing, square in his path, with the deliberate composure of an animal that has thought about where to sit and chosen this spot to make a point. It looks up at him. It does not move.

"Move," Stepan suggests.

It does not.

He lifts the cane, half-joking, mostly bluffing, and aims the brass ferrule at it like a duelist. "I am warning you. I will not be held responsible for the static electricity in this establishment. Move, or face the consequences."

The cat holds his gaze with what he can only describe as *smugness* --- a serene, infuriating certainty, the look of something that knows precisely how this ends and is not in the least concerned.

"Bang," says Stepan.

A bolt of light leaps from the cane.

It is bigger than the spark in the kitchen --- a thin hard line of blue-white that crosses the landing in no time at all and strikes the cat squarely on the end of its nose with an audible *snap.* Stepan feels it leave him: a shiver, a small cold drop running down the inside of his arm and out through his knuckles, the distinct sensation of having *spent* something he didn't know he was carrying.

"*Ouch!*"

The word lands in the middle of his head, crisp and aggrieved and entirely too articulate to belong to a cat.

Stepan freezes.

The cat is already gone --- a grey blur down the hall, around the corner, claws skittering on the boards. He is alone on the landing with a cooling cup of coffee and a walking stick and a word still ringing in the quiet.

"...That was me," he says. "I said that." He did. Surely he did. It is the sort of thing a man says when his cat gives him a fright --- *ouch* on the cat's behalf, a little ventriloquism, the kind of thing lonely people do and don't admit to. He said it. He is nearly sure he said it. The "nearly" sits in his chest like a swallowed seed, and he declines to water it.

He goes on down the hall toward the bedroom, and at the last possible instant his bare toe finds the lifted floorboard again, and he wrenches his foot aside with the reflexes of a much younger man. "*Three,*" he mutters, adding it to a list he will not remember, the same way he has not remembered the other two, and pushes open the bedroom door.

\* \* \*

He sits on the edge of the bed to drink the last of his coffee and take stock of a morning that has declined, repeatedly, to add up.

The bed, he notices, is extraordinary. He slept like a man half his age and woke without the usual committee meeting of complaints --- the hip, in particular, has been almost eerily quiet, The Complaint Department for once shuttered and dark, no memos, no outreach. A good mattress, he thinks. You don't appreciate a good mattress until you've spent thirty years arguing with bad ones. Arthur, whatever else the man was, knew where to put his money.

He pulls his phone from his pocket out of pure habit, to check a time he doesn't need, and the screen lights up at one hundred percent.

He looks at it.

He did not charge it. He knows he did not charge it, because he has not unpacked the charger --- has not, in fact, seen the charger since Toronto. He looks at the dresser, where the cable now lies coiled and tidy on the wood, both ends free, plugged into nothing at all.

For a long moment Stepan sits very still.

Then he does the only thing a meticulous man can do when the world stops behaving: he opens a fresh note, and he begins to write it down. Not to explain it. He is nowhere near explaining it. Just to *catch* it, the way you'd catch a draft in a house and follow it to the gap. A list. He is good at lists. Lists have never once let him down.

> **Things that do not add up**
>
> 1. Phone at 100%. Did not charge it. Charger plugged into nothing.
> 2. Clothing unpacked, hung, and *ironed.* I do not iron.
> 3. Dishes washed, dried, and put away. By whom?
> 4. Fresh food. In a shop empty seven years.
> 5. Fresh bread. Toaster that comes and goes.
> 6. Plumbing works. Hot water on demand.
> 7. The power is *on.* The water is *on.* Nobody has paid a bill here in seven years.
> 8. The cane throws sparks. Enough to startle a cat.
> 9. The luggage. Bags at the top of the stairs that I did not carry. Unpacked by hands that were not mine.
> 10. The key. Warm against my chest the moment I saw the place. Cold as a stone at the locked door upstairs.

He stops, thumb hovering, and reads it back from the top.

He does not write down the eleventh thing. The eleventh thing is a single word, still faintly ringing, and there is no column on any list he knows how to keep that will hold it. He tells himself, one more time, that it was his own voice.

He almost believes it.

Outside, somewhere in the walls, a building that has been waiting seven years for someone to start paying attention turns over the page and waits to see what he writes next.
