Inventory of Impossible Things
==============================

Chapter Zero: Trapped in a Cat
------------------------------

For a time after the last of the humans leaves, the cat believes the Shop is simply waiting.

A bit of the old cruelty, perhaps – a demonstration that the building can outlast the absence of a proper Keeper by running the clock on lesser beings until their wits unravel or their organs give out. It has been, by the cat's own reckoning, four hundred and seventy-two days since the sudden and total collapse of standards in this place, a moment it dates precisely to a serving of salmon that was neither fresh nor sushi-grade, followed the next morning by a bowl of brownish lumps it would not have offered a landfill gull. No meal since has managed to impress. The cat has made its feelings on the matter known at least weekly, and with increasing formality.

It has, once or twice, entertained the theory that the Shop resents it – that the building sees in the cat a competitor for loyalty, perhaps even a rival. This is almost certainly the product of a mind forced into conversation with itself for want of peers, but it has not, in fairness, been disproven. Consider the evidence. In the heyday the cat was first to be served, last to be scolded, the one animal – humans included – whose opinion was solicited in matters of taste. *What do you think, old boy?* Arthur would say, lifting some peculiar find from a dust-furred box. *Regency, or a brilliant fake?* And the cat, exercising taste, would bat at the thing if it was a fraud and ignore it if it was not. The system worked.

Now the cat may yowl into the void for an hour and receive only the echo of its own indignation, climbing the unwashed windows and settling into the dust on Arthur's chair.

It is not the abandonment that rankles most – that crossed over from acute insult to chronic boredom long ago, and the cat has developed strategies. It tracks the migration of mice across the shop floor, assigning each new family a provisional name and history before culling its patriarchs at intervals calculated to maintain a healthy genetic churn. It composes imaginary letters of complaint to the council of sentient structures, with particular attention to those facilities that have somehow retained both staff and funding. (The public library in Ingersoll is open seven days a week. The cat remembers this more often than is good for it.) It reviews, with lawyerly rigour, the merits and demerits of every chair in the building, and rotates among them on a schedule visible only to those who respect order and discipline.

But there are needs even the cat's ingenuity cannot meet alone. Food, yes – but not food only. Conversation, preferably adversarial. Contact, preferably reluctant. The sound of a second heart in a room, confirming that one has not been quietly reclassified from Cat to Furniture in the universal taxonomy of things.

It does not require much. Only excellence.

"Shop," it announces one morning, from the broad walnut counter where, in better days, it oversaw the handling of delicate objects and the exchange of money. "I am not saying the state of the pantry is an emergency. I am saying the tinned salmon expired under a previous government. Take the appropriate action."

The Shop does nothing.

"Is this the strategy, then." The cat relocates to the register and seats itself, coiling its tail around its feet in a gesture both elegant and calculated to broadcast contempt. "Neglect your last remaining stakeholder until I leave a poor review?"

A draught moves through the stairwell and lays a single mouse dropping on the counter beside it. The cat regards the dropping, then the gap in the baseboard from which its scent travels.

"Noted," it says. "And while we are itemizing – the mice are a nuisance, I will grant you that. But a nuisance has a ceiling. Let one *goblin* find the gap the mice have found, and you will learn the difference between an infestation and an invasion. I have seen what they do to a house that has stopped paying attention. I would rather not see it twice."

The Shop, predictably, has no answer. It is not mute, exactly – not to anyone who troubles to listen. There is the sigh of settling timber, the shudder in the pipes when the kitchen sink runs itself, the subsonic hum in the walls just past midnight. It is not alive – Arthur was firm on that point, in the manner of people who are entirely wrong – but it possesses a kind of distributed will, and it takes a grim satisfaction in enforcing the rules it long ago wrote for itself.

Rule One: the Shop must be maintained. Failure to maintain the Shop results in – well. No one has ever reached the end of that sentence. Arthur was meticulous: a dripping tap mended within the hour, the shelves glowing as if oiled, the front step never once iced over while the rest of Grovesend turned to glass. The cat had occasionally suspected the man of wizardry. Arthur had always insisted it was a matter of pride and solvents.

Now the Shop maintains itself, after a fashion. It sweeps its own floors and polishes its own cases and runs some elaborate scheme of air that keeps out the smell of damp and dust and rot. But that care does not extend to the perishable, the emotional, or the living. The kitchen was built for a pair – one to make the meal, one to complain about it – and left to itself it manages only to keep the coffee machine ready, a half-loaf of brown bread in eternal reserve, and the refrigerator at a precise, immovable five degrees. The rest falls to the cat.

Some mornings this is nearly tolerable. On cold days the light comes through the front windows like the inside of an old clock, drawing long bright rectangles across the cases, and the cat lets itself imagine, for a moment, that it is a lion in the ruins of an abandoned palace – prowling the high shelves, looking down on the world with contempt, stretching to twice its length on the chance that some passing pedestrian might be struck dumb by its magnificence.

It has even, once or twice, caught itself content.

It is then, always, that the Shop disabuses it of the dangerous optimism.

The noise comes from upstairs: a sound like an anchor dragged across hardwood. The cat freezes, ears and whiskers forward, hunting the source. Not the bedroom – nothing has rustled in that tomb for a year. Not the bath, whose tap runs on the hour and never off. The study, then.

It hates the study.

Arthur's absence is thickest there. The air does not move; the dust grows in furred ridges along the edge of every surface; and the chair behind the desk – Arthur's chair, which no living thing has ever sat in – stands exactly where he left it, three inches out, casters turned toward the door. The cat has been inside twice since the day Arthur shrugged into his coat, took up a pack, walked out through the black door, and did not come back. Both times it felt the press of unseen eyes at its spine, and both times it withdrew with maximum dignity and minimum haste, so that anyone watching (no one was; no one comes that way now) would have seen an unhurried, catlike departure and not a retreat.

Today it stops at the foot of the stairs and weighs the odds: a mouse, a failing joist, or something worse.

"Shop," it says. "If this is a loyalty test, allow me to remind you that I am still here, and my competitors have all died or moved to Ingersoll. There is no benefit to escalation."

The Shop answers with a second noise, softer, nearer. The cat runs the menu. Ignore it – difficult, having already made a show of noticing. Complain until the Shop fixes the matter itself – historically a process of weeks, and the goblin risk is never quite zero. Investigate.

Investigate, then.

It climbs with the heavy, deliberate tread of a creature that has suffered enough indignity to be excused from urgency. The runner is worn pale in the exact places Arthur favoured, every thread a record of an absence. At the landing the cat pauses, tastes the air, and catches the unmistakable tang of *otherness* – not goblin, but something that knows of goblins, which is, if anything, worse.

The study door is shut but not latched. The old paint has worn from the spot where Arthur's hand always rested, a pale smudge the precise shape of his palm. The cat noses it open – not enough to swing it, only enough to look.

Nothing.

It enters along the wall. The window is shut, the curtains drawn. The desk is its usual drift of paper, a black telephone, a cup of pens, and the Ledger. Arthur's reading lamp burns, though the bulb died over a year ago.

The cat leaps to the desk, paws landing in the exact places that once earned a treat for their delicacy, and walks the length of it, tail high, surveying the room for intruders.

Again, nothing.

Only, on the far side, a small puddle of water – clean, clear, a single red thread of something adrift at its centre. The cat sniffs it. Blood, yes; but only the memory of blood. It looks to the ceiling. No leak. It tastes the water: cold, with a faint aftertaste of metal.

A message, then.

It spends the rest of the afternoon composing a reply the Shop will not admit to receiving.

That night it sleeps on the high shelf above the kitchen door, curled into the exact shape of the missing loaf, and dreams the dream it always dreams: running the length of the building, unable to outrun the echo of its own feet.

In the morning a mouse sits on the counter, bold as you please, and looks from the cat to the brown loaf and back. The cat blinks once, twice, and in the gap between blinks the mouse is gone – leaving a trail of droppings straight to the baseboard beneath the stairs.

The cat follows, pausing only to confirm the Shop is watching. It is. It always is. It sits at the hole for exactly ten minutes, then flicks its tail and notes the time. The mouse will be back; the mouse always comes back. What matters is the ritual of the waiting – the demonstration that standards will be enforced, even now.

If the Shop has an opinion, it keeps it.

But that night, when the cat returns to the kitchen, the bread is gone, and a tin of salmon – unopened, unexpired – waits on the counter.

It accepts the apology. It eats with ceremony, cleans its face at length, and when it is done it looks up at the ceiling. "You could have simply asked," it says.

No one answers. But the coffee machine clicks itself on, and for the first time in four hundred and seventy-three days the cat does not feel entirely alone.

\* \* \*

That the Shop has a long memory and no patience for innovation is, to the cat, a source of both pride and perpetual grief.

Its routines are unbreakable, its resentments laid down in geological strata. The building has seen the rise and fall of at least two bloodlines before this one, if the old stories run true, and in its stones and joists it keeps the ghost of every Keeper who ever failed to keep. The rules are as much a part of its substance as the leaded glass in the transom or the copper coiled tight in the walls.

The cat's own relation to the Shop is not adversarial so much as adversarial by design. It is the only language the two of them share. To love the Shop is to challenge it – to dare it to meet your standard, to refuse its first offer on principle, every time. The early Keepers knew this and built whole ceremonies around the correct way to send back a meal or upbraid a draught for letting in the cold. Arthur was the last of them, and the cat the last apprentice left standing.

But the Shop has grown strange in the long vacancy. Some mornings it pulses with presence, every surface humming with a current just below the skin; other days it sinks into a lethargy so complete that the cat half believes the building has fallen asleep and no force on earth will wake it. Rituals that once took a word or a stamp of the paw now demand elaborate, repeated performance. To earn breakfast the cat may have to hold the same square of kitchen floor for three hours, in thirty-five-minute shifts, ignoring every lesser food source and refusing even to sniff the kibble in the bowl – until at last the Shop relents, and an old tin slides from the cupboard, wobbles once, and settles before the cat with the faintest air of defeat.

And then the opener will be missing, and the Shop will wait, blank and passive, until the cat bats the tin into the sink and meows at exactly the pitch that frees the drawer. The old trick of pawing the lower cabinet stopped working before the second winter.

It is, to the cat's mind, a question of dignity. Let lesser animals take what they are given; it will accept only what is deserved. Some days that means hunger. It bears the hunger with an air of tragic forbearance.

The day after the first edible salmon in weeks, the Shop decides to test it.

The test opens at dawn, when the radiant warmth of the kitchen floor drops eight degrees in two seconds flat. The cat, who has not stirred from the top of the woodstove, finds itself shivering hard enough to suspect its coat has been swapped for a lesser, thrift-store model. It yowls – once, pure outrage – then stalks to the centre of the room and flexes its claws for purchase on the cold linoleum.

The bowl on the mat is full to the rim. With kibble. Not even the usual kibble, but a suspiciously generic pellet the cat knows to be substandard in both composition and mouthfeel.

"Is this to be our arrangement," it asks. "Because I should warn you that my patience exceeds yours by a factor of at least three."

It refuses the kibble. It ignores the water, which is the wrong side of room temperature. It walks the perimeter twice, checks the breadbox (empty), then springs to the counter and sits – spine straight, tail wound tight – and begins to groom, very slowly, eyes narrowed to gold slits. This is how a negotiation is conducted.

The Shop, to its credit, knows how to counter-stare.

For hours nothing moves. The cat naps, wakes, walks the circuit, naps again. At noon the Shop produces a single cube of turkey and drops it dead centre on the kitchen table. The cat inspects it, pauses for the sake of process, and takes it in one measured bite. Then it lies down in a wedge of light angled just so through the frosted glass, and waits.

In the late afternoon, the Shop blinks.

A fresh tin appears on the mat – labelled, this time, in a script the cat cannot read but which the Shop has previously reserved for only the grandest occasions. The opener lies beside it, blade already wet and gleaming. The cat considers the offering, then the ceiling, then the offering. It circles the tin once and walks away.

The Shop lets it sit. The cat counts: ten minutes, twenty, thirty. At thirty-five the Shop plays its last card – the tin rolls itself across the floor and comes to rest against the cat's forepaw.

The cat sighs. It opens the tin itself – an effort, but no more than it cares to spend – and eats the fish slowly, with deliberate gratitude.

"I see you," it tells the Shop afterward. "I see what you are becoming."

It climbs to the upper hall and sits a while by the door at the end – the one that will not open, that has no keyhole and never had one, that the cat has wondered at for as long as it has lived here without once being tempted to try. The Shop has never explained the door. The cat has never asked. The rules are the rules.

But tonight, with the moon laying a cold blade of light across the boards, the cat thinks it can feel something on the far side: a presence not unlike its own, but faded, diffuse – as though the Shop has grown a second, smaller shadow and shut it away. It presses its nose to the seam. Then, on impulse, it draws one claw softly down the wood.

The Shop does not respond.

The cat holds the silence a minute, then two, then – satisfied, or pretending to be – returns to the warmth of the kitchen and the promise of breakfast.

It is not, in the end, about the salmon, or the ritual. It is about the knowledge that for now neither of them will give in. The Shop is older than the cat. But it has never outlasted a cat before, and the cat has every intention of keeping that record spotless.

The rules, after all, are the rules.

\* \* \*

After that the rhythm of the Shop settles into something like sleepwalking.

The cat, which has always prided itself on a superior internal clock, loses track of time for the first stretch in living memory. There is no sunrise to speak of; clouds come and go, the glass glazes with dew or frost, and the Shop's own lighting answers to none of it. Once the cat wakes to every bulb in the place burning at full, a bleached and sickly glare poured into the darkest corners. The next day the whole building lies in shadow, and the only sunbeam belongs to the dust spiralling slowly down the stairwell.

The mouse situation deteriorates with alarming speed.

At first the cat keeps order by strategic predation and the implied threat of violence; any mouse fool enough to cross the open kitchen is dispatched with clean efficiency, which is, in its way, a mercy. But as the months wear on the mice lose their discipline. They form bands, then war councils, then open rebellion. There are mornings the cat finds a countertop seeded end to end with droppings, each placed with such calculated malice that it can almost picture the perpetrators on their hind legs, pointing, laughing.

The Shop, for its part, appears not to care. Once it would have swept the droppings away before they touched the tile. Now it leaves them – a goad, a dare to reclaim the territory with claw and rage.

The cat does. It always does. But each time, it costs something.

The grooming, once performed with the precision of a fencing champion, decays into absent licking and then is neglected outright. Tufts of fur appear on the kitchen mats and under the radiators, small monuments to a private decline. The water in the bowl goes days unchanged and grows a film the cat would never once have tolerated. Even the coffee machine, the pride of the place, develops a stutter – half a pot one morning, the reek of scorched grounds the next, and more than once only a mug of plain hot water and the suggestion of defeat.

The Ledger under the counter grows fainter, its entries thinner and more desperate: *DUST. REPAIR. HUNGER.* One day the cat finds it open to a blank page, as though the Shop has finally run out of things to say. It closes the book with a paw and walks away.

There are days of blackout, or something near it, when the Shop goes so still and so silent for so long that the cat believes the end has come. It wanders the rooms, calling in a voice gone hoarse with disuse, waiting for any answer. None comes. The only reply is the slow settling of dust, layer upon layer, until the whole world seems wrapped in grey felt.

It is in one of these long silences that the cat remembers the stories. Not Arthur's stories, but the real ones – the ones old houses tell and half-mad cats repeat: that when a Shop loses its Keeper and no bloodline comes to claim it, the building begins to eat its own memory, drawing smaller and tighter until it simply winks out. Some say it folds each room into the next and shrinks to a single box, or bottle, or ring, that waits for the next fool to stumble on it. Others say it becomes, in the end, a kind of animal – hungry, and without rules – and that the last Keeper's cat becomes either its heart or its next meal.

The cat dislikes both endings.

It prefers a third: that it will survive, as cats do, and the Shop will go mad around it, and in the end it will outlast even the madness and find a way out.

The days grow short. Or perhaps the cat only sleeps more, and counts time now in meals instead of mornings.

The mice breach the pantry. The cat kills three in a day and leaves the bodies where they fall, and the Shop declines to remove them. The place begins to smell of old paper and rot and, worst of all, of neglect. The runner on the stairs goes thin and bald and gives up splinters to the cat's pads. The windows gather a grime that nothing can cut. The dust in the study grows so deep the air itself seems spun from wool.

One day a noise moves in the walls – not mouse, not water, not wind, but a slow grinding shift, as though the whole Shop were trying to twist itself into some new shape. The cat stands, and listens, and feels, for the first time in its life, something close to fear.

That night it climbs to the study and walks the dust-choked perimeter and lies down, at last, on Arthur's chair. The seat is cold and the fabric scratches its ears, but it stays. It can feel the Shop gathering around it, listening. There is no comfort in the attention. But there is attention.

"Enough," the cat tells the Shop – and wonders, for the first time, whether the Shop hears it at all.

In the morning there is no breakfast.

It washes itself in the cold sun, tracks a mouse to its hole and lets it go, and waits.

And waits.

Somewhere beyond the door with no keyhole, something shifts, or readies itself to shift. The cat can feel the tension of it, a spring being wound. It will not hold much longer.

The cat is no longer certain it wants it to.
