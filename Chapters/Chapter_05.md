Inventory of Impossible Things
==============================

Chapter Five: The Account
-------------------------

There is a particular satisfaction, Stepan is discovering, in being
shown how to do a thing correctly and then being left alone to do it.

Danica shows him the first tread, supervises the second, and by the
third has retreated to the bottom of the stairs with a mug of the
hazelnut she is pretending not to be delighted by, calling up
corrections he mostly doesn't need. He pries the dead board, fits the
new one, and drives the screws so they bite. And each time a tread comes
right under his hands, the small warm coal turns over behind his
breastbone - there and gone.

He has stopped pretending not to feel it. He has instead started to
notice *when*. Not when he rests, not when he eats. Only when he sets
something right. Cause, he thinks, and effect. He does not yet have a
theory. He has merely stopped insisting there is nothing to have a
theory about, which, for Stepan, is a substantial concession, roughly
equivalent in other men to a religious conversion.

Prince supervises from the landing with the air of a foreman who
suspects the union. *You're over-driving that one,* it observes, into
the middle of his skull. *You'll strip the -*

He over-drives it and strips the hole.

He says nothing. He has become, in the space of four days, a man who can
be heckled by a cat in front of company and merely smile, and he is
privately rather proud of the discipline - which Prince finds
significantly more irritating than a reply would be.

But the cat is not really working today. Between jibes, it keeps
slipping off down the stairs, to the same dim stretch of baseboard at
the front of the shop, where it sits and stares at nothing and will not
be coaxed away. Twice Stepan catches it there, dead still, ears forward,
and the stillness bothers him more than any amount of heckling.

"That's the lot," Danica says at last, rapping the new treads with a
knuckle. She gathers her tools, waves off his thanks with the ease of a
woman who has been thanked too often for too little, and is gone in a
growl of the F-150 down the lane - leaving the shop very quiet, and the
afternoon going gold at the windows.

\* \* \*

Alone, Stepan can finally answer the cat. It feels less like madness now
and more like a bad habit he has decided to stop apologizing for.

"All right," he says, lowering himself – easily; the hip files no
objection worth the name – to the floor beside the corner that has held
Prince's attention all day. "What is it?"

Prince does not gloat. That, more than anything, prickles the back of
his neck.

*Put your hand near it,* it says. *Not in it. Near.*

He holds his palm a few inches from the gap where the baseboard has
pulled away from the floor - a gap he had filed, vaguely, under *mouse*,
under *list*, under *later*. The air coming out of it is wrong. Not the
dusty exhale of an old building, not a draught off a cracked foundation.
It is cold - a flat, mineral cold he has felt exactly once before, on
the landing upstairs, at a door with no keyhole and a key gone to ice
against his chest. And beneath the cold rides a smell that has no
business in Ontario, or possibly on Earth: something green and electric
and faintly rotten, a thunderstorm over a tidal flat.

"That's not a mouse," he says.

*No,* says Prince. *It is not a mouse. It is a way through, and it is
getting wider, and I have been watching it get wider for longer than you
have been alive to a single fact about this place.* The voice has none
of its usual ornament. *Mice come through it. Mice are nothing - they
are how I know it's open. It is what comes after the mice that I have
lost sleep over.*

"After the mice," he repeats.

The cat turns its ridiculous amber eyes on him, and for once, there is
nothing performed in them at all. *You'll see,* it says. *And I am sorry
that you will.*

It is the gentlest thing the creature has ever said to him, and it is
how he knows to be afraid.

\* \* \*

It comes through at dusk.

He has been sitting with the gap for an hour, the way a man sits with a
problem he can't solve, and he has very nearly argued himself back to
*mouse* - *the cold is a draught, the smell is damp, I am a tired old
man building siege engines out of rain the way Arthur taught me* \--
when the baseboard shivers, and a hand comes out of it.

It is not a human hand. It is grey-green and long-fingered and the size
of a child's, with too many knuckles, and it grips the lip of the gap
and *pulls* - and the gap gives, widening with a wet tearing sound, and
a thing levers itself out into the front room of Arthur's Curiosities
and crouches there, blinking, in the last gold light.

It is small. That is the first thing, and the only mercy. Perhaps the
size of a large cat, all elbows and gristle, its skin the colour of pond
scum, its eyes flat and black, its mouth far too crowded with teeth. It
smells of the gap. It does not rush him. It crouches, and it looks at
him, and then it looks at the shelves with their century of crowded
objects, and something that is unmistakably *appetite* crosses its small
and terrible face.

Stepan does not decide to move. His body decides for him - the same way
it decided about the chair the first time a mouse ran, except the thing
in front of him now is not a mouse, the cane is already in his fist, and
he is not climbing anything. He brings the brass ferrule around, and
there is a *crack* of blue-white and the goblin shrieks - a sound like a
kettle flung down a staircase - and scrambles, not back through the gap
but sideways, around the shelving, horribly fast.

What follows is not a battle. It is an old man and a furious cat chasing
something small and vicious around a cluttered room, and it is
undignified, and it costs him three strikes - *three* - to end it: the
cane snapping light, the goblin shrieking, Prince a grey streak driving
it back into the open every time it breaks for cover. The first strike
scorches it. The second drops it. The third, when it drags itself
upright and comes on anyway, all teeth, is the one that stops it for
good.

And then it is over. The thing is not moving. The room reeks of ozone
and pond and something scorched, and Stepan is standing in the wreck of
a toppled shelf with a walking stick in his hand and his heart going
like a trip-hammer.

\* \* \*

He waits to feel triumphant. He has read enough to know this is the part
where the hero feels triumphant.

He doesn't.

What arrives instead, all at once and far too large to hold, is
everything the goblin *means*. It was real. It was *real* - which makes
the gap real, which makes the cold door upstairs and the warm key and
the book he is half-sure has been writing about him all real as well;
not the charming eccentricities of a clever old building but the actual
furniture of a world he has somehow been made responsible for, in a
transaction he does not recall agreeing to. There is a thing that comes
through the wall. It is, apparently, *his* to stop. No one asked him.
There is no one left to ask.

And then there is the part he did not see coming - the part that puts
him down. He has just killed something. On every rational level, he does
not regret it: it was vicious, it meant harm, it would have done worse
to him given the chance. But it had hands. It had a face that wanted
things. It shrieked when it burned. He is sixty-eight years old, and he
has gone a long, careful, unremarkable life without ever once ending a
living thing larger than a wasp, and he finds - standing over the small,
ruined body of a creature that would gladly have gutted him - that the
ending of it has cost him something he cannot name and did not expect to
spend. It is not grief. It is not guilt, quite. It is the quiet,
permanent knowledge that he is now a man who has done this, and will, he
suspects, do it again.

His hands are shaking. His legs lodge a formal complaint. He looks
around the dim shop for somewhere to sit and finds, as ever, that the
building has left him exactly one option: the tall stool behind the
front counter, an island of dust in a room it otherwise keeps. He
crosses to it and sits down heavily and puts his face in his hands. And
the cat - who has, for once, the decency to say nothing at all - springs
up onto the counter beside him and leans, briefly, the whole warm weight
of itself against his arm.

\* \* \*

It is a long while before he lifts his head, and then only because there
is nothing else to do with his eyes but look at what sits in front of
them, which is the counter.

The counter is the one surface downstairs that the shop has never once
tidied. He has noticed this before and filed it, the way he files
everything, under *later*. But he is sitting at it now, wrung out and
emptied and disinclined to move, and a man with shaking hands and
nothing to do will, eventually, begin to pick at the nearest thing. He
drags a finger through the dust. He squares a cup of dead pens. He
slides open the shallow drawer beneath the till - receipts gone soft
with age, a tin of fused mints, a magnifying glass.

And then his knee, swinging idle against the counter's underside, knocks
something solid back in the kneehole - in a pocket of shadow he notices,
now, is darker than the dimness around it has any right to be. The way
that corner has always seemed, somehow, *reserved*.

He reaches in. His hand closes on a book.

\* \* \*

It is heavier than its size - bound in leather aged to the colour of old
bark, its corners capped in tarnished brass, its cover worked with a
pattern of interlocking shapes that will not quite hold still under his
thumb. It is warm, which books are not.

He sets it on the counter and opens it, and the spine yields with the
soft reluctance of a thing that the right hands have not opened in a
long time.

The early pages are a wall of cramped script in inks gone brown - hand
after hand after hand, names and dates and entries he cannot parse in
the failing light, a record that runs back further than the building has
any right to. He turns past decades. He turns past a long, blank,
dust-coloured stretch where nothing was written at all.

And then he reaches the new ink.

It is black, and faintly iridescent, and it was not - he is certain -
written by him, though it is unmistakably *about* him, in a dry,
clipped, faintly editorial hand he has never seen and somehow already
knows:

> *A nail, reseated. Upstairs hall. (The first thing he has put right.)*
>
> *Crockery washed and shelved, unprompted. (Noted. Cautiously.)*
>
> *Floorboard; non-slip strip; one stair tread, stopgap. (He is
> learning.)*
>
> *Three treads, replaced - with assistance. (The assistance was
> discounted, per the usual arrangement, on the grounds that it was not
> of the house.)*
>
> *Dust disturbed in the study. (We will allow it.)*

And then, last, in ink still wet enough to hold the light, an entry that
stops his breath:

> *One intruder, dealt with. Three attempts. (We will work on that. He
> did not enjoy it - good. A guardian who enjoys it is a different and
> worse problem. He'll do.)*

Stepan sits very still, in the gold gone grey, in the quiet shop, with a
dead goblin behind him and a warm book beneath his hands, and reads it
through again.

The shop has been *watching*. The shop has been keeping count - every
small thing he has set right, from the first nail to the body cooling on
the boards - and the count is not idle. *He is learning. He'll do.* The
repairs were never only repairs. They were being added up, toward
something, by something, and the something has decided, tonight, in a
parenthesis, that he will do.

He should feel watched. Managed. Ledgered, counted, quietly conscripted.
He does. But under it, unwelcome and undeniable, is the other thing -
the thing he has not felt in the long careful narrowing of the years
since anyone last needed him: the sense of having been handed a job that
*matters*, by something that was paying attention.

"You could have mentioned this," he tells the cat.

*I mention a great many things,* says Prince, from the dark at the end
of the counter. *You are only now in any state to hear them.*

Outside, the last light leaves the windows. Behind the wall, faint and
patient, the cold comes on through a gap that is wider tonight than it
was this morning. And on the counter, beneath an old man's hands, a book
that has waited seven years for someone worth writing about turns, of
its own accord, to a fresh and empty page - and waits to see what he
does next.
