Inventory of Impossible Things
==============================

Chapter Nine: Vermin
--------------------

Danica arrives the next morning with a paper sack of groceries the shop does not need and a verdict already formed.

"You," she announces, setting the sack on the freshly dusted counter and looking him over the way a vet looks over a horse she suspects of malingering, "look disgustingly good."

"Clean living," Stepan says, which has become his answer to everything.

"You looked seventy when you got off that train. You do not look seventy now." She squints. "Did you have work done? Out here? Who would you even go to, Ferdy at the garage?"

"It's the country air."

"It's something." She says it lightly, and lets it drop, and he is grateful, because the truth – that a building is quietly mending him the way he mends its floorboards, paying him back in years for the work – is not a sentence he can say aloud to a woman holding eggs. He pours her a coffee from the monolith, thumbs the hazelnut button she pretends is beneath her and drinks every time, and they eat at the counter like two people who have known each other longer than a week.

Prince conducts the meal from the stool's edge, fixing Danica with the unblinking amber stare it has decided works on her, and is rewarded, against all of Stepan's stated policy, with a corner of bacon.

*She is a soft touch,* the cat observes into the middle of his skull. *Cultivate her.*

Stepan says nothing because he has learned not to answer the cat in company. He has discovered this is a rule the cat finds endlessly exploitable.

\* \* \*

After breakfast, Danica wanders the front room the way she always does, touching nothing, noticing everything, and stops at the baseboard by the old gap.

"Now this," she says, crouching to run a thumb along the new board he set on the worst night of his life, "is decent work. Tight. You sealed your mousehole." She sounds faintly proud of him, as of a slow student come good. "See, I told you. Steel wool and a board and they give up and go next door."

"They do seem to have given up," Stepan agrees, which is true in the sense that the thing he stopped is dead and folded into the wall behind the very board she is admiring.

Then she straightens, and her eye travels along the wainscot toward the back of the shop, and snags.

"You've got another one."

His stomach goes down a slow, cold elevator. He follows her pointing finger to the dim stretch of baseboard near the rear shelves, where the wood has gone soft and grey, and the dark beneath it is, to anyone who has learned to see it, not the dark of a wall. He had not noticed it. He has been so busy with the front of the shop that he never looked at the back, and the shop, he thinks, has kept him from looking, the way it keeps him from looking at a great many things until he is ready.

"Bigger, too," Danica says, with professional disapproval. "That's not a mouse, that's a rat run. You've got rats, Novacek." She frowns at it. "Old building like this, near the fields. I'm honestly surprised it took this long."

"Rats," says Stepan. "Wonderful."

\* \* \*

She insists, of course, on doing something about it then and there, and he cannot think of a way to refuse that does not sound mad, so he kneels with her at the back wall and performs the theatre of pest control.

It is a strange thing to pack steel wool into a hole that opens onto somewhere with teeth. He does it anyway, going through the motions she names – wedge the wool, tack a scrap of board, note it for a proper fix – while every nerve he owns reports that the cold breathing out of this gap is wrong, is the mineral-and-rot cold of the other place, and stronger at the back here than it ever was at the front. The board he tacks over it feels less like a repair and more like a sheet over a face.

From the end of the aisle, Prince watches with its fur standing in a ridge from skull to tail, and it does not pretend to be a cat.

*That will not hold,* it says, flat and certain. *That is not the front gap. The front was a crack the small ones widened. This one was made wider from the other side, on purpose, by something with a plan and a shoulder to put behind it. Your wool and your board are an insult it will be glad to answer.* A pause. *When it comes – and it is coming – it will not be a stray. Be ready in a way you cannot be while she is here.*

Stepan would dearly love to answer. He would like to ask *when,* and *how big,* and *what do you mean, a plan.* What he does instead is grunt, sit back on his heels, and say to Danica, "That should hold them a while," in the voice of a man who believes it.

"Mm," says Danica, who is wiping her hands and already moving on, and over her shoulder, the cat gives him a look of such withering disappointment that he has to turn a laugh into a cough.

\* \* \*

They break for the afternoon over ice cream.

He does not, strictly, make it. He opens the squat machine that appeared in the night two days ago and finds it has produced, against no instruction, two small bowls of vanilla at exactly the moment two bowls are wanted. He carries them out and says nothing about provenance, and Danica takes hers and says, around the first spoonful, "You made this? Get out. You churned ice cream?"

"I have hidden depths," says Stepan, which is the most honest thing he has said all morning.

They sit. The shop is warm at the front, and quiet, and for a while they talk – the eaves, the weather coming, a feud at the township office she narrates with relish. And then, the way these things turn, she is talking about Arthur.

"I met him, you know. Once or twice, when I was a girl." She turns her bowl slowly. "He was already old by then, or I thought he was. Tall, though. Stood up straight as a door, none of that folding-in old men do. And he had a walking stick –" she gestures at the ash cane leaning against Stepan's stool, and something in Stepan goes very still – "a bit like yours, come to think of it. Heavier. Covered in carvings. Little scratches all up the shaft, like writing, except not writing you could read. I asked him once what they said, and he told me they said *mind your business,* and gave me a butterscotch." She smiles at the memory. "Here's the funny thing, though. My mother knew him thirty years before that, and she always said he looked exactly the same. Going grey, always going grey, never quite getting there. Never getting *old.* Just – tall, and grey, and then one day gone."

Stepan keeps his face very still and his voice very mild. "Gone where?"

"That's the question, isn't it?" She shrugs, and the moment passes, and she scrapes her bowl. "People around here'll tell you he walked off on one of his trips and didn't come back. He was always going on trips, Arthur. Never said where." She stands, stretches. "Anyway. He'd have liked you, I think. Same kind of strange."

Stepan does not answer that either. He is looking at his own cane, leaning unremarkable against the stool, and thinking about scratches that look like writing, and a man who went grey for sixty years and never got old, and a great many small impossible things arranging themselves, very quietly, into a shape.

\* \* \*

Danica leaves in the late afternoon with a list of her own and a promise.

"I'll swing back after supper," she says, swinging up into the F-150. "I've got proper warfarin at the house and a fresh roll of wool. We'll bait that back hole tonight and stuff it right, none of your finger-in-the-dike nonsense. Get on top of it before they're in the walls for good." She points a finger at him through the open window. "Don't argue. Arthur let this place go to the rats and look what it got him."

"I wouldn't dream of arguing," says Stepan, and means it, though not for her reasons, and watches the truck growl off down the lane into the gold of the failing day.

The shop is very quiet when she has gone.

He stands in it a while, listening, and then he hears it – or rather feels it, the way you feel a window left open somewhere in a house. The cold has started again at the back wall, steady and patient, sliding out from behind a tacked board and a fistful of steel wool, pooling along the floor toward his feet. It is stronger than this morning. It is stronger by the minute.

Prince comes to stand at his ankle, fur up, and says nothing at all, which says everything.

Stepan looks at the back of the shop, where the dark is breathing through his useless little patch, and at the cane leaning against the stool with its unreadable scratches he is only now beginning to wonder about, and he reaches out and takes it up.

"All right," he says, to the cat, to the shop, to whatever is gathering on the far side of the wall. "Let's not be here when she gets back."

It is, he will reflect later, the single most optimistic thing he has ever said.
