Inventory of Impossible Things
==============================

Chapter Four: A Work of Fiction
-------------------------------

It is a cat.

That is the first thing, the load-bearing thing, the thing it repeats to itself the way a prisoner repeats the layout of a cell. It is a cat. It has fur and four feet and an indecent fondness for cream, and if anyone – anyone at all – were to look at it and think otherwise, were to say the other word aloud, the true word, the one buried so deep it does not allow the word into its own skull, then the door that is currently merely shut would become a door that is locked, and the lock would have no key, and that would be that, forever.

So. It is a cat. It has been a cat for seven years, in this shop, among the dust and the dead man's clutter, talking – because talking is the one thing they could not take – to walls, to mice, to the procession of the bewildered and the unwelcome who occasionally rattled the black door. Talking, and being heard by precisely no one, because that is the rule of the thing: the only ears that can catch its voice are ears with the right blood behind them, and the right blood had walked out of this shop seven years ago and not come back.

Which is why, kneeling in the hall yesterday with a hammer in his graceless paw, when the old man *answered* – 

The cat had gone very still. It is still going very still, in its way, a day later, watching the lump of him snore in the grey morning light.

Because the old man answered. A muttered nothing about how one holds a hammer, and the old fool had argued the point. Out loud. With *it.*

The cat is not a fool, whatever its current dietary arrangements suggest. It lines the facts up like a hand of cards. Only the blood can hear. The old man heard. Therefore the old man is the blood. And the blood in this shop, the blood the shop has spent seven sulking years waiting on, is one line and one line only – the dead man's line, the line of the uncle, of *Arthur*, who used to sit in the study upstairs and take his tea with milk and talk to a cat as if it were a person and never once, not once, suspect he was right.

*Oh,* the cat thinks, with the particular horror of the very clever arriving very late. *Oh, you idiot animal. It's the nephew. It's Arthur's own blood, come to mind the shop.*

And that – that is the most dangerous thing that has happened to it in seven years. Worse than the cold. Worse than the canned food. Because a keeper who can hear it is not a comfort. A keeper who can hear it is *hope,* and hope is the thing that makes you careless, and careless is the thing that turns a shut door into a locked one. It has survived this long on a diet of cream and zero expectations. It cannot afford the old man. It cannot afford to want anything from him at all.

It pads downstairs in the dark while he sleeps, to be sure, because being sure is free and being wrong is not. The Ledger sits under the counter where it always sits, in its pool of slightly-too-deep shadow, and the cat puts its front feet up on the shelf and looks. There, in ink that was not in the book a week ago – a deep, wet, iridescent black – a tidy column of small deeds. *Nail. Dishes. Floorboard. Strip. Tread. Dust.* The shop is keeping his account. The shop has decided. The shop is never wrong about blood.

The cat sits with this for a while in the dark.

Then it goes back upstairs, arranges itself on the foot of the bed it has decided is now partly its own, and resolves, with the iron discipline of royalty in exile, to give absolutely nothing away. It will eat his food and insult his coffee and complain about the litter. It will be, in every observable particular, a cat.

It is so much easier to be a cat than to be the other thing.

\* \* \*

Stepan wakes, eventually, to the conviction that he has solved everything.

He has not, of course. But he has solved the *shape* of it, which for a meticulous man is nearly as good, and he lies on his back working the solution like a man working a splinter loose. The problem is not that the cat talks. He has, regrettably, accepted that the cat talks. The problem is what a person *does* with a talking cat – specifically, how a person lives in the world, buys groceries, speaks to a livery driver, and is not, by lunchtime, fitted for a pleasant jacket with very long sleeves.

The answer arrives over a reheated plate of yesterday's bacon, and it is so elegant he nearly laughs.

He will be a writer.

Not a real one – or, well, real enough. He is a man of sixty-eight with a lifetime's reading behind him and a dead uncle's shop full of impossible things around him, and if anyone should ask why he talks to himself, or mutters at the cat, or stops mid-sentence to stare at nothing as if listening – *I'm working on a novel.* A book. About a talking cat, as it happens. Writers are permitted, even expected, to be a little cracked; eccentricity is the one occupational hazard nobody questions. He can say the maddest thing in the world out loud, and as long as he says it in the tone of a man composing chapter four, the world will simply nod and leave him a wide berth.

"You," he informs the cat, who has materialized on the kitchen table to supervise the bacon, "are research."

*I beg your pardon.*

"My protagonist. A talking cat. Snide. Entitled. Strong opinions about cream." He points the fork. "I'll change a few details so you don't sue."

The cat's tail lashes once. *I am not material.*

"Everything's material," says Stepan, who has not written so much as a postcard in forty years and is enjoying himself enormously. "That's the first thing they teach you."

\* \* \*

The good humour lasts until the cat tries to steal his lunch.

It is a clean, professional theft – a feint, a flow, a paw already closing on the rasher – and Stepan, without thinking, without even deciding to, flicks the cane up off the table edge and *taps* the air toward it.

*Tick.* The cat takes the spark square on the nose and goes straight up, all four feet, an outraged grey firework, and lands three feet away radiating betrayal.

*You – you absolute – * The voice in his head is incandescent. *I am owed bacon. I am owed a very great deal of bacon, retroactively, and you – *

"House rules," says Stepan, and feels the small cool drop run out through his knuckles, the now-familiar sense of having spent some coin he can't see, and does not, this time, bother to say the word *static,* because he is too busy laughing, and because – he notices, filing it under the long list of things he is not ready to think about – the cat is looking at him afterward not with simple feline grievance but with something measuring. Something that has done arithmetic and not liked the sum.

\* \* \*

Danica's truck comes up the lane a little after noon, and she comes in.

That, Stepan thinks, is itself a small event. He has watched this shop hold the world at arm's length for two days – the warm key, the cold door, the way the very dust seems to have opinions – and he half expects the building to do something to keep her out. It doesn't. But the black door, which has swung wide and willing for him since the first hour, sticks for just a beat under Danica's hand, a half-second of resistance, before it gives. She doesn't notice. He does. The shop, it seems, will permit a guest. It simply wants noted, for the record, that permission was required.

She has brought the fish, wrapped in paper and packed in ice, and a canvas roll of tools, and the brisk competence of a woman who has fixed more things than she can count. She crouches at the bad stair, runs a thumb along the rot exactly as he had, and says, "Yeah. This one's done. Whole tread's got to come out."

"I did wonder if I could –"

"You could not," she says, not unkindly, and starts laying out tools.

And here is where Stepan, who has been waiting all morning for an audience, makes his mistake. Because he wants, very badly, to show someone the trick – the mad, marvellous static-electric trick, proof that he is not losing his grip but living in a genuinely peculiar house – and the cat is *right there,* sitting on the third step with the smug repose of minor nobility.

"Watch this," he says, and points the cane. "Damnedest thing. Static, the whole place is full of –"

Nothing happens.

He points it again. He gives it a small encouraging shake, the way you'd shake a torch with a loose battery. The cat regards him with serene, bottomless contempt and does not move and is not, in any way, zapped.

"...It does it sometimes," Stepan says.

"Mm," says Danica, who has gone back to the stair, and whose opinion of his sanity he can feel quietly recalibrating.

*Perhaps,* observes the cat, into the privacy of his skull, with the leisurely cruelty of the truly comfortable, *it only performs for an audience of one. Like a great many disappointing things.*

He does not answer. He has resolved, with what he privately considers heroic discipline, not to answer the cat where anyone can see him do it – it warned him itself, the others can't hear it, they'll only hear him – and so he stands there, a large man holding a stick, swallowing a retort while a cat insults him and a competent woman pretends not to watch him fail to electrocute a house pet. It is possibly the longest thirty seconds of his retirement.

And then the mouse runs out from under the shelving.

It is a small brown ordinary mouse, and Stepan – broad-framed, heavy-set, a man who has faced down hospital boards and Toronto traffic and the collected disappointments of six decades – makes a sound he will deny for the rest of his life and is, with a speed his hip will bill him for later, standing on a chair.

"It's a *mouse,*" says Danica, delighted.

"I'm aware of what it is."

"It's about the size of a thumb."

"I'm aware of its *dimensions* –" The mouse, panicking, breaks straight for him across the open floor, and Stepan does the only thing a man on a chair can do, which is brandish the one weapon he has, and the cane comes down and *cracks* – 

 – a hard whip of blue-white light, far brighter than any spark he's struck off the cat, lancing down and catching the mouse mid-stride. It drops. It does not get up.

The silence afterward is total. Danica is staring at the small scorched spot on the floorboards, and at the cane, and at Stepan, with the expression of someone whose understanding of an afternoon has just been revised.

"...Static electricity?" Stepan offers, from the chair.

\* \* \*

Danica, to her enormous credit, decides – visibly, and with the air of a woman choosing the easier of two roads – not to pursue it. There are things, her shrug says, that a person who drives the Grovesend roads simply files under *that place,* and a sparking cane in a shop that stocks its own fridge is apparently one of them. She disposes of the mouse with farm matter-of-factness, washes her hands at the impossible sink without remarking on the impossible hot water, and gets to work, and Stepan – climbing down from the chair with what dignity remains to him – gets to work beside her.

She does most of it. He will not pretend otherwise, and neither, at length, will the Ledger. But she shows him things – how to lever the dead tread without splitting its neighbours, how to read the grain, how to drive a screw so it bites and holds – and he does some of it himself, hands remembering an aptitude he'd forgotten he owned, and each time a piece of the shop comes right under his fingers he feels that small warm flicker behind the breastbone, the one he no longer bothers to deny, the shop saying, in the only language it has, *yes; this; thank you.*

The cat supervises from a height, and keeps up, for Stepan's ears only, a running review of the proceedings – the quality of Danica's work (grudgingly adequate), the quality of Stepan's (a comedy), and, when Danica bends to fit the new tread, a single observation so vulgar and so accurate that Stepan has to turn a bark of laughter into a cough.

"Bless you," says Danica.

He does not answer the cat. He is getting better at not answering the cat. It is, he is beginning to understand, going to be the great discipline of his old age.

\* \* \*

By evening the stair is sound, Danica's truck has growled off down the darkening lane, and Stepan is so tired that the prospect of cooking strikes him as a cruel joke. But the fish is there, and the fish, he has been given to understand at length, is not optional, and there is exactly one way to serve it.

"You realize," he tells the cat, laying the pan on the heat, "that an actual cat would eat this raw and be grateful."

*An actual cat,* the cat replies, from its station on the counter, *would also drink from the lavatory. I am not interested in the standards of actual cats.*

So he cooks the fish. And he plates a portion for the cat and a portion for himself, and – because he is too tired to fight about the table, and because some part of him has started, against his every instinct, to enjoy the company – he sets the cat's down on the floor and his own at the counter and they eat, the two of them, in something that is almost, nearly, companionable.

It lasts until the cat finishes and decides the portion was an insult.

The rant that follows is a masterpiece. It takes in the size of the serving, the indignity of floor-level dining, the temperature of the cream that should have accompanied it, the general decline of standards in a house that cannot even keep out vermin, and the specific, premeditated character failings of a host who would zap a starving creature off a single rasher of bacon. It is delivered in flawless, withering paragraphs, gathering grandeur as it goes, and Stepan – full, exhausted, every joint composing its own complaint – listens to the whole imperial aria with his chin in his hand, and when it finally pauses for breath, he says:

"You know what? You know what, *Your Highness?*" He sets down his fork. "I'm giving you a promotion. From this day forward you are no longer Annoying Cat. You are *Prince.* Because you are, without exception, the most royal pain in the ass I have ever had the misfortune to feed."

The cat freezes.

It is only a heartbeat – less – but Stepan, who has spent a lifetime cataloguing the small tells of the people across from him, catches it whole: the way the animal goes rigid, the way the amber eyes blow wide and stricken, the way something that is unmistakably *terror* crosses its face, raw and instant, as though the word has reached in and closed a hand around something it was hiding. For that one heartbeat the cat is not a cat at all. It is something cornered, and caught, and afraid.

Then it is gone. The mask drops back so smoothly he could almost believe he imagined it.

*Prince,* the cat repeats, recovering, packing every ounce of disdain it owns into the single syllable. *How perfectly droll.* And it rises, and turns, and stalks from the kitchen with its tail straight up and its hindquarters presented to him in the most eloquent insult in the feline language, and is gone into the dark of the shop.

Stepan sits a moment longer.

"...Right," he says, to the empty kitchen, and rubs his face, and adds it – quietly, to the list he keeps now only in his head, the one with no paper and no eleventh line and a great deal of white space below it – that for half a second there, a cat had looked at a joke about a name as though it were a death sentence.

He is too tired to chase it. He goes up the sound stairs, on his good hip, and is asleep before the thought can finish.

\* \* \*

The shop waits until his breathing goes slow and even, the way it always does.

Then, in the dark kitchen, on the smooth blank face of the coffee monolith, a second button rises out of the surface – quietly, without ceremony, the way a thing surfaces in still water – and settles, and is, by morning, as though it had always been there. Somewhere in the deeper dark a Ledger nib scratches once and goes still. And on the cold hearth in the front room, a grey cat that is not, whatever it tells itself, entirely a cat sits with its tail wrapped round its feet and watches the building rearrange its kindnesses, and does not sleep, and gives nothing away.

\* \* \*

Morning comes clean and ordinary, and Stepan notices the ordinariness the way you notice a tooth that has stopped aching.

He comes down the stairs and no tread shifts and no toe is stubbed; the floor of the hall lies flat and obedient; the stair he and Danica rebuilt takes his weight without a murmur. The shop, mended a little, behaves. He stands at the bottom for a moment, faintly suspicious of how unremarkable it all is, and then goes to make breakfast, because a man has to eat whether or not his house has started cooperating.

Prince arrives at the smell of bacon and lodges a formal demand for more of it.

"You've had yours."

*I have had an amount. There is a difference, which your kind has never grasped, between having had an amount and having had enough.*

The truck is in the lane again before Stepan has finished the eggs – Danica, with a longer canvas roll this time and a flat box of parts, and the unmistakable expression of a woman who has come to enjoy a show. "Brought treads," she says, setting them down. "There's three more on that staircase about to go the way of the first. Figured I'd supervise while you do 'em." She says *supervise* with relish.

"Coffee?" Stepan offers, retreating to the one duty he understands, and it is only as he reaches the monolith that he sees it: the second button, sitting in the smooth black face as if it had grown there overnight, which – he is now prepared to believe – it has.

He looks at it for a second. Then, because some forms of surrender are easier with company, he decides not to mention that it wasn't there yesterday.

"It does hazelnut now, apparently," he says instead. "Hazelnut, if you like that crap."

"*Hazelnut?*" Danica lights up like the sun has come out. "That's my favourite. How'd you –"

"Lucky guess," says Stepan, and presses the button, and a ribbon of hazelnut coffee runs out hot and fragrant, and Danica makes a small involuntary noise of pleasure, and somewhere down by the floor a grey voice says, with vast satisfaction, *Oh, I like that one. A little of that, in a clean dish, would not go amiss* – and Stepan, who cannot answer, who must not answer, who is hemmed in on one side by a delighted woman and on the other by an entitled cat and squarely in the middle by a coffee machine that grows buttons in the night, says nothing at all, and pours.

They take it to the table. Prince eats, under loud protest, at floor level. Danica maps out where a person buys lumber and where a person buys groceries and which of the three stairs is worst, and Stepan finds a scrap of paper and a pencil, and the two of them walk the front room together in the thin morning light, and begin, item by ordinary item, a list of everything in the shop that needs putting right.

It is, he thinks, looking at it – the loose sash, the cracked pane, the shelf gone to lean – a very different sort of list than the one he started yesterday on his phone. That one had been a record of things he could not explain. This one is a record of things he can fix.

He decides he likes this one better.

Across the room, ignored by the humans and tracked by no one but the reader, Prince pauses at a dark gap low in the baseboard where the wall meets the floor – sniffs it, once, with sudden and uncharacteristic focus – and says nothing at all to anyone.
