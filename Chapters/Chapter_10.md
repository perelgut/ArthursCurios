Inventory of Impossible Things
==============================

Chapter Ten: Charged
--------------------

He does not turn on the lights. Some instinct older than reason tells him the dark is better for this, that a lit shop is a shop announcing its keeper, and so he stands in the gold ruin of the last daylight with the cane in his fist and waits at the back wall for the thing he sent Danica away to spare her.

The cold comes off the second gap in a steady exhale now, no longer the thin draught of a crack but the open breath of a door. His tacked board and his fist of steel wool bulge faintly outward, mocking him. He has, by his own count, exactly one trick in him – the amber rune of *Magic Missile* hangs ready at the edge of his eye, recovered across the quiet days – and after that he has a stick and an old man's arms, and he is under no illusion about how far either will carry him.

*This is a foolish way to do this,* Prince says, low and tight, fur a ridge along its spine. *Alone, in the dark, with one shot. You should have let her bring her poison and her wool and gone to bed.*

"You said it wouldn't hold."

*It will not. I said you should be ready in a way you cannot be while she is here. I did not say to fight it on your own with a walking stick.* The cat's tail lashes once. *You are not as expendable as you have decided to be.*

He has no answer to that, which is its own kind of answer, and then there is no more time for answers, because the board splinters.

\* \* \*

It does not scramble through like the small ones. It *comes* through – a grey-green arm, then a shoulder, then the whole of it heaving the gap wider by main strength and stepping into his shop as though it has every right.

It is bigger than the brute. Worse than bigger: it is *armoured*, plates of some dark, scaled stuff lashed across its chest and shoulders, a notched blade of black metal in one fist, and it does not blink in the gloom, and it does not rush. It looks at him the way a soldier looks at a sentry; it expects to kill on the way to somewhere more important.

Stepan does the only thing he knows how to do. He levels the cane, and he pours the cold flat *no* down the ash and out through the brass, and the world clears its throat.

The missile takes it square in the chest – and the armour *shatters*, blows apart in a spray of black scale and a sound like a tree coming down, and the thing is flung back two steps, reeling, stunned.

It does not die.

He has time, in the ringing afterward, to understand the size of his mistake. The door-breaker broke the door it was wearing. That is all. And as the horror of *that is all* lands, the air in front of his face fills with amber, unbidden, insistent, scrolling faster than he can read –

> **Magic Level 2.** *Constitution increased.* *New working acquired: Reflect Ward.*

– a level risen on the back of one desperate shot, the system flooding back into him, rearming, offering, explaining, at the precise and catastrophic moment he has no attention to spare for any of it. He registers none of it but the colour. He is already reaching for another missile he does not believe he has.

\* \* \*

The bell over the black door jangles.

"Novacek, I forgot the –" Danica stops in the doorway with a paper sack in one arm and a fresh roll of steel wool in the other, and the sack hits the floor, and the warfarin spills across the boards in a little white hiss, and her voice goes somewhere he has never heard it go. "*What is that.*"

Everything in him drops. She is here. She is *here,* in the worst doorway in the world, and there is no version of the next ten seconds in which he gets her back out of it.

"Stay behind me," he says, and is distantly amazed at how level it comes out.

The warrior shakes the ruin of its armour off its shoulders, and finds its feet, and fixes its flat black eyes on the two of them with something that is no longer a soldier's indifference but an animal's enraged attention – and it comes, fast and low and furious, the black blade up.

\* \* \*

He reaches for the missile, and there is nothing there – he is sure there is nothing there, his hands and his terror agree there is nothing there – and so he does the other thing, the new thing, the word the amber tried to give him that he half-heard without hearing: he throws up the ward.

He does not know how. He wants, fiercely, *not to be struck,* and the wanting goes out of him and hangs in the air between him and the blade like a held breath –

– and the warrior strikes, and the blow comes back.

It is the strangest thing he has ever felt and not felt: the blade lands on nothing a foot from his chest, and the force of it folds *back,* and the goblin is flung from its own blow, staggering, a long scorched gash opening down its arm where its own strength turned on it. The held breath collapses. The amber where the ward was winks out, spent.

Danica makes a sound behind him. Her hand finds the small of his back and grips a fistful of his coat, not for his sake, he thinks, but because the floor of the world has tilted and he is the nearest fixed thing.

"Stepan," she says, very quietly, "what are you."

"Later," he says, which is the cat's word, and means it the same way: *yes, and you are not ready, and neither am I.*

\* \* \*

The warrior gathers itself at the edge of the wrecked shelving, bleeding its own blood now, and it is hurt, and it is humiliated, and it is between them, and nothing, and Stepan understands with total clarity that he is out. No missile he can find. No ward left hanging. Sixty-eight years old, a smashed board behind him and a terrified woman gripping his coat, and the only thing left in his hands is a length of ash with a brass foot, a walking stick, a plain dumb stick.

It comes.

"Can you do something?" Danica says, fast, desperate, her hand pressing flat between his shoulder blades. "Stepan – *can you fight it?*"

And something answers her question that is not Stepan.

It starts where her palm meets his back: a warmth, then a brightness, then a flood – a clean silver-white light pouring out of her and into him, through him, down the length of his arm and into the wood as he brings the cane around in the only swing his body has left. He feels the stick *wake* in his grip, feels it go from dead weight to something with a will and an edge, and the brass ferrule meets the side of the warrior's skull with a sound that does not belong to wood at all.

The goblin drops. It does not twitch. It is, instantly and completely, dead.

The silver light fades. Stepan stands over the body with the cane thrumming faintly in his hand, and turns, and Danica is staring at her own palm in the dimness as though it belongs to a stranger, lit from within by the last of a glow she did not summon and cannot explain, her face white, her mouth open on a question she has no words for.

\* \* \*

For a long moment, neither of them says anything at all.

Then Stepan, because someone has to, says the true thing, plainly, the way you say a hard thing to someone you have decided to stop protecting: "You have magic. It's real, and it's in you, and it just saved both our lives." He hears how it sounds. He says it anyway. "I think – I'm fairly sure – you're what the old stories would call a cleric. A different kind from mine. Divine. Yours comes through your hands."

Danica looks at him as though he has begun speaking Latvian. "A *what.*"

"A cleric. It's –" and here he founders, because he has spent sixty years with this vocabulary and she has spent none, "it's a class. A type. There are levels, and – characteristics, attributes, the things you're strong in – yours would be the divine ones, Wisdom, the –"

"Stepan." Her voice is flat with shock and rising. "I don't know what any of those words mean. Levels. Characteristics. I never played the – whatever it is, the dragons, the dice. Nobody did. Not in Grovesend, not in Ingersoll, not anybody I ever met in my life." She looks at her hand again. "I poured light out of my body into a stick, and you're telling me about *attributes.*"

And he understands, standing there, that the system he has been quietly delighting in for a week – the amber, the *Status*, the tidy familiar grammar of the thing – is his and his alone, a private language he happens to have studied his whole life without knowing it was a language. To her, it is not a game come true. It is only terror, with no manual.

"I'm sorry," he says, and means it. "I don't have answers. I have – worse than no answers. I have a vocabulary, and it's useless to you."

Behind him, on the counter, in the dark, a pen-nib begins to scratch.

\* \* \*

He knows the sound now. He crosses to the kneehole where he hid the book two days ago and draws it out into the open, because there is no hiding anything anymore, and he sets it on the counter, and it is already writing, the wet black ink crawling across the page in its dry, unhurried, editorial hand. And there are two entries.

The first is about him – he sees his own week summed in a line, the count of his small repairs tipped at last across some threshold he didn't know he was approaching, a quiet notation that he has, in the language of the house, come up in the world. The Charisma the system just granted him, he supposes; the standing of a man people will, from now on, find themselves listening to.

It is the second entry that stops his breath, because it is not about him at all.

> *A second bloodline now stands in the shop: divine, ungoverned, and entirely untrained. (It is about time a cleric returned and took up some responsibilities. We had begun, frankly, to despair.)*

Danica reads it over his shoulder – he feels her go rigid – and reads it again, and says, in a small voice with nothing left in it but bewilderment, "It's writing about *me.* The book is *writing about me.*"

"It writes about everyone in the room," Stepan says. "I'm only now learning how little it misses." Try saying the word Status – say it as if you're demanding to see your status from a doctor or teacher.

Danica looks at him like he's crazy but says, "Status!" Instantly she starts to shriek and swallows it because she has never been the type of girl who shrieks when surprised. In front of her is a silver display, overlaid on her vision. Stepan can't see it but can tell that she can. He says, "Just say Clear with the same conviction."

Danica practically shouts, "Clear!" and everything is back to normal. Then, "Status" again. Then, "Clear" and "Status". Now, she asks what all these things mean, and Stepan tries to explain. It's getting to be too much. She sits, stunned. "What does Bless Object mean"? Stepan says, "I'm guessing but that's what you did to save us both. You blessed my poor walking stick and it somehow delivered the final blow. You probably have experience assigned also. That's the XP." Danica looks at nothing and says, "it says I have 1 out of 20 points needed for L2. What does that mean?" "You've already earned your first level, probably from helping around here and from the blessing. Do you have something else besides Bless Object in the writing."

She looks exhausted and weirded out and says, "Yes, something about Detect Evil. What does that mean?" Stepan tries to explain. He thinks that, maybe, she found the second hole because her spell was working by default. It's a long explanation, and most of it seems to be going around Danica without being absorbed. Eventually, she just says, "Clear" in a weak, tired voice and asks, "Is it safe now?"

Stepan blushes and says, "I have to ask the cat." She looks at him, thinking, "More crazy!" Stepan turns to Prince and says, "If we stuff it back and reset the boards, can we sleep soundly?" Prince replies, though only Stepan can hear, *Yes, there wouldn't be more than one warrior assigned to a hole this small and insignificant. Eventually, more will try to come, but not for days, I think*.

Danica looks at Stepan as he tells her what the cat said. She absorbs the "being safe" part, looks at Stepan, and says, "I need to go home and sleep on this." Stepan helps her off the chair and walks her to the door, trying to make her feel like this is just a different sort of normal – but it isn't, and they both know that. Danica leaves, and Stepan locks up before he sets about moving the dead ... warrior ... it can't be anything else. Once stuffed back, he sees no blood stains – the Shop is good for that much. So he stuffs in the remaining Warfarin, plugs the hole with steel wool that's not going to help very much, and gouges odd marks on a board that he nails to the wall – feeling that same surge of having done that right thing. That done, he sits in the kitchen.

At his feet, the cat sits and watches him and the open book and where the dead thing lies on the floor, and says nothing whatsoever – which Stepan has learned to read as the loudest thing it can say. It knows. It has always been known. *That is a longer conversation,* it had told him once, *and not entirely mine to have,* and he sees now that it was never only talking about him.

\* \* \*

The shop is very quiet, and very cold, and through the partially blocked, torn gap at the back, the dark goes on breathing, patient, unhurried, because a soldier is only ever sent ahead of an army.

Stepan looks at the wider wound that he just covered up in his wall – not a mouse-hole, not a crack, but a *door,* widened from the far side by something with a plan – and thinks about the dead armoured thing that walked through it as though expected. Then he thinks of Danica, white-faced but glowing and changed forever in the space of a minute, and at the book that has been keeping its dry, devastating count of all of them since long before he stepped off the train.

He came here to settle an estate. He is, instead, the keeper of a failing wall between his small warm shop and something vast and cold and coming, with one spell and a half-woken staff and a woman who pours light from her hands and cannot say the word for what she is.

"All right," he says again, to the cat, to the shop, to the open book, to the breathing dark. It is becoming a kind of prayer with him, that phrase, the only one he has. "All right."

It is not all right. But it is, he is beginning to understand, the job.
