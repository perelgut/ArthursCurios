Inventory of Impossible Things
==============================

Chapter One: The Key to Arthur's Curios
---------------------------------------

Stepan Novacek has made several questionable decisions in his
sixty-eight years. He once bought a timeshare during a thunderstorm, ate
gas-station sushi outside Barrie on a dare he had issued to himself, and
tried to assemble Swedish furniture without reading the instructions, on
the working theory that how complicated can shelves be. Boarding an
elderly train out of Toronto because a dead uncle has left him a shop
ranks, he thinks, impressively high on that list. Possibly the podium.

The carriage offers no counterargument. It shudders along the tracks
with a rhythmic clack-clack that travels up through the floor, into the
seat, and directly into the hip Stepan long ago promoted from Minor
Annoyance to The Complaint Department. The Complaint Department keeps
terrible hours, posts no schedule, and maintains an alarming commitment
to customer outreach.

He shifts his broad frame against a seat designed by someone who plainly
resented broad frames, and glares at the paper cup of commuter coffee in
his hand. Every lurch sends a dark wave slapping the lid and, now and
then, a scalding droplet onto his knuckles, just to keep the
relationship honest. The coffee tastes like punishment administered by
committee. But it is hot, it is bitter, and it is technically liquid, so
the railway has met its obligations and intends to hear nothing further
on the matter.

His cane rests wedged between his knee and the seat ahead: a thick,
unvarnished length of ash with a heavy brass ferrule at the bottom. It
is less a mobility aid than a formal warning --- to stairs, to uneven
pavement, and to anyone under thirty who believes "just a quick walk" is
a harmless thing to say to a man holding a stick.

The certified letter from the estate lawyer lies folded in his coat
pocket, as smug and unavoidable as a tax audit. Beside it sits the key:
large, antique, all dark iron and ornate teeth, the sort of key that
implies whatever it opens has never once considered a sensible deadbolt
from Canadian Tire. Uncle Arthur --- missing seven years, absent from
the family for nearly forty --- has at last been declared legally dead,
and, in the grand tradition of eccentric relatives, has reached out from
beyond the grave for the express purpose of being inconvenient.

And yet Stepan cannot quite manage to resent him. Arthur was the
brilliant storyteller of his childhood, the sort of man who could turn a
rainy afternoon into a siege, a cupboard into a portal, and a single
missing sock into compelling evidence of goblin activity. His tales of
hidden kingdoms and ambulatory houses and small, subtle magics ruined
Stepan for ordinary fiction before he was ten. As a boy, when his
allowance failed to keep pace with his appetite for dragons, doomed
prophecies, and suspiciously well-described sorceresses, he haunted the
used bookstores until he acquired a deep tolerance --- if not outright
affection --- for dust and structural mildew.

Now, apparently, he has inherited the dust. Progress, he reflects, comes
in many forms, most of them disappointing.

\* \* \*

By the time the train wheezes into Ingersoll, Stepan has negotiated
terms with his hip: he will rise slowly, and it will refrain from open
rebellion. He grips the cane, levers himself upright, and shuffles down
the aisle while the younger passengers perform the ancient ritual of
studying their phones with sudden scholarly intensity, lest they be
conscripted into helping an old man with a bag.

Outside, the air is crisp and smells of damp earth, diesel, and rural
confidence. The station's red-brick building sits primly beside the
tracks, historic in the way small towns deploy the word when they mean
old, photogenic, and absolutely not up to modern wiring code.

Being a meticulous man, Stepan has not left the final leg to chance. He
pre-booked a local livery to carry him from the station to Arthur's shop
in Grovesend, because improvisation is for jazz musicians, teenagers,
and people with better knees. The confirmation email was brief,
practical, and signed simply, *Dan*.

A woman leans against a Ford F-150 at the curb, arms folded, one boot
crossed over the other. She wears a canvas work coat, her dark hair
clipped back, and has the sort of easy good looks that earn a man a
courteous, entirely reasonable moment of appreciation. Stepan is
retired, not deceased, and there is, to his knowledge, no statute of
limitations on noticing symmetry.

Then he remembers he is waiting for Dan, and looks past her for the
grizzled farmer he has assembled out of three words of email: plaid
jacket, weathered cap, practical boots, a name shared with either a
tractor or a minor Old Testament prophet. The woman's mouth curves.
Apparently his inner monologue has sprung a leak.

"Looking for someone?" she asks.

"Dan," Stepan says, adjusting his grip on the cane. "Local livery. I was
told he'd be---"

She pushes off the truck and walks straight past his outstretched hand
to seize both suitcases. Stepan had a three-act drama prepared about
lifting them --- interval, intermission, medical disclaimer --- and
watches her heft them like mildly judgmental pillows.

"Dan's short for Danica," she says over her shoulder, already striding
toward the truck. "And I'm your ride to Grovesend."

He stares after her for half a second, then sets off in pursuit with as
much dignity as a man can summon while being outpaced by his own
luggage. The cane taps sharply on the pavement. His hip files a protest
and copies the knee for visibility.

By the time he reaches the passenger door she has loaded both cases into
the bed and dusted her hands, wearing the expression of a person who
solved the problem while he was still locating the intake form.

"Pop yourself in," she says. "I pushed the seat back for legroom."

"Very considerate," Stepan says. "Also very fast. Do you offer pursuit
training, or is that extra?"

Danica grins. "Depends how much luggage you bring."

He climbs in and sinks into the seat with a sigh so sincere it deserves
witnesses. Danica pulls out of the lot and heads east, and the last
edges of town surrender to rolling fields, old fence lines, and maples
that look as though they have been judging passersby since
Confederation.

"Grovesend doesn't get many visitors," Danica says, "unless they're
lost, farming, or making a poor life choice with real confidence. Which
are we working with?"

"Inheritance," Stepan says. "An old relative left me a shop. Arthur's
Curiosities."

Her foot eases off the accelerator. Only for a heartbeat --- but he
notices. Retired men with unreliable joints collect few advantages;
clocking the precise instant a person regrets a conversation is one of
them.

"Arthur's place?" she says. "You're Arthur's nephew?"

"That seems to be the allegation," he says. "Did you know him? Tell me
about Arthur, and the shop."

"Nobody really knew Arthur." She turns down a narrower gravel road.
"Quiet. Odd. Always polite. As for the shop --- that's where it gets
interesting."

"I was hoping for 'mildly profitable,'" Stepan says, "but legend will do
in a pinch. Legends at least imply foot traffic. Some of it living."

Caution tugs at the edge of her smile. "It's been shut since he
vanished. Seven years. Around here an empty building usually gets broken
into by teenagers, raccoons, or teenagers doing an unconvincing raccoon.
Arthur's place, nobody touches."

"An antique shop immune to vandalism," he says. "There's a business
model in there somewhere. Add a coffee machine and you could franchise
it."

"Kids won't go within a block after dark. They say things move inside
when nobody's there. One boy threw a rock at the front window and swears
it stopped dead in the air, dropped straight onto the porch, and broke
his toe. The council sent a building inspector once. His truck died
every time he pulled up, and started again only after he got towed past
the town line."

"That sounds less haunted than aggressively municipal," Stepan says.
"Which, I admit, may be the more frightening option."

Danica smiles, but her eyes stay on the road. "Folks think the place has
a mind of its own. Likes its privacy."

He gives a low laugh. "A haunted antique shop. Wonderful. I had hoped
for a modest pension and one armchair that understood me on a spiritual
level. Instead I inherit Scooby-Doo with property taxes."

Her expression warms, though the caution does not leave it. "Maybe. But
out here, some old places know how to look after themselves. Five
minutes out. Hope you're ready for dust."

"I've been training for dust my whole life," Stepan says. "At last, the
relevant skill set."

As the pickup crests a long hill, Grovesend appears in the valley below:
a handful of rooftops tucked beneath ancient trees, a crossroads, a
church spire, and the distinct impression that the whole hamlet was once
forgotten by accident, rediscovered later, and politely asked not to
make a fuss about it.

At the edge of the crossroads sits Arthur's Curiosities.

Dark, narrow, and weathered, the shop leans beneath its sagging sign
like an old man pretending he has not been waiting for company. The
windows are filmed with grime. The door is painted black. And as Stepan
watches from the passenger seat, the iron key in his coat pocket grows
warm against his chest --- which is not, in his fairly extensive
experience, a thing keys are encouraged to do.

He decides it is the coffee. It is not the coffee. But the decision
holds him steady until the truck rolls to a stop.

\* \* \*

The truck idles at the curb while Stepan considers the building, and the
building, he suspects, considers him back.

Up close, Arthur's Curiosities is even less reassuring than it looked
from the hill. The clapboard has weathered to the grey of driftwood and
old regret. The sign above the door hangs from one good hook and one act
of faith, the lettering faded but stubbornly legible, as though it has
decided that being read is a matter of principle. The windows are filmed
with a grime so even and so complete that they manage the neat trick of
being opaque from the street while presumably letting Arthur, in his
day, watch everyone who approached. The door is painted a black that has
surrendered its shine but none of its conviction.

"Well," Stepan says. "It has character."

"That's one word for it." Danica has already swung out and dropped the
tailgate. She sets his cases on the porch --- not beside the door, he
notices, but a careful half-step back from it --- and returns to the
truck without lingering in the doorway's shadow. For a woman who carries
luggage like an accusation, she has become suddenly, deeply interested
in being somewhere else.

Stepan levers himself down onto the gravel, plants the cane, and finds
the step up to the porch costs him less than he budgeted for. He files
this under generous legroom and a long sit, and declines to examine it
further, on the principle that examining small mercies is the surest way
to frighten them off.

"You're not coming in," he says. It is not quite a question.

"I'm a livery service, not a tour guide." Her tone stays light, but her
eyes flick to the black door and away again, the way a person checks a
dog they have been assured is friendly. "You've got my number on the
confirmation. If the place turns out to be more than you signed up for,
call. I'll come as far as the porch." A beat. "I mean that as a genuine
offer and a genuine limit."

"Noted on both counts," Stepan says, and means it. He has spent a
lifetime listening to what people decline to say, and Danica is
declining a remarkable amount.

She climbs back into the F-150. The engine catches on the first turn of
the key --- he notices that too, after the story about the inspector ---
and she lifts two fingers off the wheel in a wave that is somehow both
friendly and final. The truck crunches back up the gravel until
Grovesend settles into the particular silence of a place built before
traffic and never persuaded of its merits.

Then it is only Stepan: two suitcases, a cane, and a shop doing an
extremely convincing impression of patience.

The key has not cooled. If anything it sits warmer in his pocket than
the short walk from the truck can explain, a small steady heat against
his ribs like a coal that has forgotten to go out. He fishes it out and
turns it over. Dark iron, ornate teeth, the heft of something forged
with strong opinions. It is warm the way a stone is warm after a day in
the sun, except there has been no sun, and it is entirely the wrong
season for that particular excuse.

"Static," he informs the key. "Or my heart finally filing its
paperwork."

The key declines to comment.

The lock is a great black plate of a thing, its keyhole shaped for
precisely this key and, he would wager, no other in the world. By every
reasonable expectation a mechanism this old and this neglected should be
seized solid --- the sort of lock you fight for ten minutes and resent
for an hour. Stepan braces for the fight. He slots the key home, sets
his shoulder, and turns ---

--- and the bolt draws back in one long, oiled, unhurried motion, smooth
as a sentence finishing itself.

He stands there with his hand on the key and his prepared struggle
suddenly homeless. "Well maintained," he says, to nobody, in the firm
tone of a man insisting on a thing he has just been handed excellent
reason to doubt.

The door swings inward before he has entirely decided to push it.

What comes out to meet him is not the wet reek of a building shut up for
seven years. There is dust, yes --- but beneath the dust, old paper and
beeswax and something faint and mineral he has no name for, cool and
clean, like the breath of a deep cellar or a church between services. It
is the smell of a place that has been waiting, and dusting only the
parts that mattered.

Past the threshold the shop is dim and far deeper than the narrow front
has any right to promise, shelves receding into a brown gloom his eyes
refuse to find the back of. Somewhere in that dark a single floorboard
settles with a soft, deliberate creak --- the sound, Stepan thinks, of a
house shifting its weight to get a better look at its visitor.

"It's an old building," he says, firmly. "Old buildings settle. They do
not have opinions."

He takes up the nearer suitcase, draws one breath of that cellar-cool
air, and steps inside to be wrong about that for the rest of his life.

\* \* \*

The inside of Arthur's Curiosities is a museum that has stopped
pretending it has a curator.

Shelves run back into the gloom in no order Stepan can identify, crowded
three deep with the sediment of other people's lives: tarnished
candlesticks, a barometer that has given up forecasting anything, a
regiment of porcelain shepherdesses missing a crook, a sheep, or a face
variously. A stuffed owl regards him with the glassy disapproval of the
recently dusted. Most of it is junk --- good junk, the honest kind, the
sort that smells of attics and estate sales and other people's
grandmothers.

Most of it.

Once, somewhere between the barometer and a tray of mismatched buttons,
the back of his neck prickles the way it used to in the good bookstores
--- the ones where you might find a true first edition shelved spine-in
among the water-stained book-club editions. He turns. A snow globe sits
on a low shelf, its small blizzard already settling, though he is
certain he has not touched it and equally certain the floor has not
shaken. He looks at it. It looks, in the way inanimate objects manage
when a man is tired and the light is poor, back. He moves on, because
one has to draw a line somewhere, and *negotiating with a snow globe* is
a reasonable place to draw it.

He does not, yet, have the language for what he is half-seeing: that
most of this shop is exactly the honest clutter it appears to be, and
that a few things on these shelves are emphatically not, and are
content, for now, to wait their turn.

A door at the back, half-open, gives onto a kitchen --- and here the
shop's careful neglect simply stops, as though it ran out at the
threshold.

The room is small, clean, and improbably warm. Stepan crosses to the
sink almost without deciding to, the way you test a thing you have
already half-disbelieved, and turns the tap. Water comes --- clear,
cold, immediate --- with none of the brown apology a pipe owes after
seven years of standing. The basin is white. Not grey, not stained, not
freckled with the orange lacework of old rust. White, the way a thing is
white when it was scrubbed an hour ago by someone who took the job
personally.

"Somebody's been keeping the place up," he says, and waits to believe
it. The house declines to produce maintenance records.

On the counter stands the one object in the room that refuses, on
principle, to explain itself. It is a coffeemaker only in the sense that
coffee comes out of it: a smooth, dark monolith about the size of a
bread bin, its surface unbroken but for a single spout and a single
round button. There is no tank to fill, no hatch for grounds, no carafe,
no clock --- and no cord, he checks, because he is that sort of man,
running to any outlet in the room. He tips it forward an inch to look
beneath it. Nothing. It is a solid block of mystery with a button, and
the button is, frankly, daring him.

The cupboard above yields a tidy congregation of dishes, drinking
glasses, and mugs --- all of them clean, none of them dusty, arranged
with the quiet confidence of a kitchen that fully expects to be used. He
takes the nearest mug, a cheerful green and pleasant in the hand, sets
it beneath the spout, and presses the button before his good sense can
lodge a formal objection. A thin ribbon of coffee runs into the mug ---
hot, dark, unhurried --- and stops, with uncanny precision, an inch from
the rim. No grounds. No water. No power he can account for. He lifts it
and drinks.

The coffee is, of course, perfect. He is beginning to find the
perfection itself faintly menacing.

He carries the mug back out onto the shop floor because if the building
intends to behave like this, he means to do his sightseeing properly
caffeinated. He sets it on the edge of a display case to lift the lid on
a box of daguerreotypes, browses, prods, makes the small disrespectful
noises of a man appraising a stranger's taste --- and when he reaches
for the mug again, it is not there.

He looks. He retraces. He returns, at length and against his better
judgment, to the kitchen, where the goblin-green mug sits in the
cupboard among its fellows: rinsed, dried, and put away, as though it
had never been disturbed and rather resented the suggestion.

Stepan stands with the cupboard open for a considerable moment.

"I put it away," he announces, to the kitchen, with the firmness of a
man signing a document he has not read. "I am sixty-eight. I put things
away and forget I have done it. That is the entire tragedy of the age."
The kitchen, wisely, does not argue the point.

It is only then --- mug in hand for the second time, refilling out of
pure stubbornness --- that he actually looks at the thing he is holding.
On the green glaze, in cheerful white, grins the unmistakable skull of a
goblin king, crown and all, and beneath it, in a font built exclusively
for novelty, the legend:

*MY OTHER MUG IS A GOBLIN KING'S SKULL.*

Stepan laughs out loud, once, surprised into it. It is the first
entirely uncomplicated thing to happen to him all day. He can hear
Arthur in it --- Arthur, who turned missing socks into goblin sieges,
who would absolutely have owned a cupboard's worth of these and handed
the matching set out at Christmas to relatives who did not get the joke
and were too polite to ask. For a moment, the strangeness of the place
loosens its grip, and the shop is only a shop, the eccentric leavings of
a beloved madman.

The moment lasts until he turns around.

A cat is sitting in the kitchen doorway.

It has not, to Stepan's certain knowledge, been there a moment ago, and
there is no door behind it whose opening would account for it. It is
large --- improbably large --- and built along the lines of something
that has won every argument it has ever started: a battle-notched tom in
a coat of silver-grey that catches the poor light and does something
with it ordinary fur does not. Its eyes are amber-gold, unhurried, and
fixed on Stepan with an expression no cat has any business owning. It is
not the wary flinch of a stray. It is the cool, unhurried, top-to-bottom
assessment of a creditor, or a customs officer, or a small monarch
deciding whether the visitor may be permitted to go on existing.

"Ah," Stepan says. "You'd be the resident. I did wonder who'd been doing
the dishes."

The cat declines to confirm or deny. It simply looks at him, with the
patience of a thing that has been waiting a good deal longer than seven
years and intends to keep waiting until he understands something it has
no intention of explaining.

Stepan, who has spent a lifetime naming things to cut them down to a
manageable size, considers the bearing, the contempt, the sheer unearned
majesty of the animal --- and declines to dignify it.

"Cat," he says. "That is all you are getting until you start paying
rent."

Something crosses the animal's face --- brief, precise, and far too much
like affront to belong on a cat, as though a grander title had been
expected and was now being conspicuously withheld. Stepan blames the
light. He is getting good, today, at blaming the light.

"*Annoying* Cat, if you insist on the formal version," he adds.

The cat closes its eyes, slowly, in the manner of one enduring a great
and entirely familiar indignity. And Stepan --- a mug of impossible
coffee cooling in his hand, a dead man's joke still warm in his chest
--- has the distinct and unwelcome sense that he has just lost the
opening round of a game he did not know he had agreed to play.

\* \* \*

By the time Stepan thinks to find somewhere to sleep, the afternoon has
gone the soft grey of an Ontario evening deciding whether to bother with
a sunset.

He goes looking for his suitcases. The first he is fairly sure he left
propped against a display case on the shop floor; the second is on the
porch, where Danica set it down a careful half-step from the door.
Neither is where he left it. He stands in the middle of the shop and
conducts the slow inventory of a man who has spent the entire day being
gently contradicted by a building, and arrives, eventually, at the foot
of the staircase.

His suitcases are at the top of it. Both of them. Side by side, squared
up, as tidy as a bellhop\'s silent reproach.

On top of them sits the cat, front paws together, tail curled over them,
regarding Stepan down the length of the stairs with the serene
proprietorship of a creature that has rearranged the furniture and is
waiting to see whether he will dare to comment.

\"I carried those up,\" Stepan informs it. \"Obviously. I carried them
up, and I have forgotten, because I am sixty-eight and the day has been
long.\" He hears how it sounds. \"It is a perfectly ordinary failure of
memory.\"

The cat says nothing, which is somehow worse than if it had.

He sets a hand on the rail and starts up, braced for the usual
negotiation --- the hip\'s objection on the first step, the knee\'s
editorial on the third, the small humiliating pause at the landing while
his body decides whether to go on. The cane finds each tread. He climbs.

And the negotiation does not come.

Oh, he feels his age; he is not floating up the stairs like a man in a
soap advertisement. But the climb gives him markedly less grief than
stairs have any right to --- less than the stairs in his own apartment,
which he has been losing to by degrees for a decade. He reaches the top
with breath to spare and a hip that has filed only a token complaint,
more from professional habit than conviction. He stands on the landing,
faintly affronted.

\"The sitting,\" he decides. \"A good long sit in a truck, decent
coffee, no stairs all day. The body surprises you.\" It is a reasonable
theory. He notices, with mild discomfort, that he is assembling rather a
lot of reasonable theories.

The cat, displaced from its throne, pours down off the suitcases and
away along the landing with the unhurried disdain of a landlord who has
shown you to your room and considers the remainder of the conversation
beneath him.

Several doors open off the upstairs hall. The nearest stands ajar on a
bedroom, and the bedroom stops him on the threshold.

It is clean. Not preserved-clean, not museum-clean, but
tidied-an-hour-ago clean: the bed made up with the quilt turned down at
one corner, the pillows plumped, the window cracked a sympathetic inch
so the air smells of cut grass and evening rather than of a room shut
for seven years. A lamp burns low on the nightstand. A glass of water
stands beside it that has no business being fresh, and is, when he
checks, exactly cool enough to drink.

\"Arthur kept a tidy house,\" he says, and does not believe himself, and
is getting used to the sensation.

The bathroom across the hall continues the argument. The fixtures are
old, but the porcelain is white, the mirror unfogged and unspotted, a
clean towel folded over the rail with the geometric smugness of hotel
housekeeping. He turns the tap; hot water comes at once, without the
cough and rattle of pipes remembering their purpose. He shuts it off and
studies his own face in the unspotted glass: an old man in a shop that
is laying out comforts for him the way one would for an expected and
slightly fragile guest.

The study is the exception, and the exception tells him something.

It waits at the end of the hall, and where the rest of the upstairs has
been quietly, insistently tended, the study has been left exactly,
deliberately, alone. It is Arthur\'s --- there is no question of that
from the moment Stepan steps in. Papers drift across a vast desk in
geological strata. Books stand in towers that have developed a lean. A
cold cup of tea sits at the desk\'s edge with a skin grown over it, set
down mid-thought seven years ago and never reclaimed. The dust here is
real and undisturbed, thick and grey on every surface, and the air holds
the specific smell of a particular man --- pipe smoke, old paper, and
something Stepan\'s chest recognizes before his mind does, so that he
has to stand in the doorway and breathe through an ambush of grief for a
man he barely knew and apparently misses anyway.

The shop has tended the whole of the upstairs for him and has not
touched this room. He files the observation without forcing it into a
conclusion: that the building can plainly tidy whatever it pleases, and
has chosen to leave Arthur\'s clutter precisely where Arthur left it.
Some things, it seems, even a house knows better than to put away.

There is one more door.

It stands at the dim end of the hall, past the study, and it is shut in
a way the other doors are not. Stepan tries the handle out of the reflex
that makes a man test a stranger\'s gate. It does not turn. There is no
give in it at all --- no rattle, no play, no suggestion of a lock he
might coax or a swollen frame he might shoulder --- only a door that has
decided, with the flat finality of the front door\'s exact opposite,
that he is not coming through. He looks for a keyhole. There is none.

He takes out the great iron key anyway, because it is the only key he
owns and the day has lowered his standards, and holds it up to a door
with nowhere to put it. The key, for the first time since the hilltop,
is cold.

Behind him, down the landing, the cat has stopped. It sits facing the
shut door from a careful distance, making no move toward it, its amber
eyes fixed on the wood with an expression Stepan cannot read and is not
certain he wishes to. For a moment none of them moves: an old man, a
cat, and a door that the most accommodating building in Ontario has
elected to keep shut.

\"Right,\" Stepan says, to break it. \"We will come back to you.\"

The door, reasonably, does not answer. But as he turns toward the
bedroom and its turned-down quilt, he carries the strong and unwelcome
impression that it has noted the promise, and means to hold him to it.

\* \* \*

The bedroom is four steps away, the quilt is turned down, and Stepan
very nearly makes it.

What stops him is the doorframe. He is the kind of man who cannot walk
past a crooked picture, and the trim on the bedroom door is worse than
crooked: a finishing nail has worked its way half out of the wood, and
the long strip of casing has begun to bow away from the frame, a slow
wooden shrug that in a year or two will split. It offends him the way a
typo offends him --- immediately, and out of all proportion.

On the hall table --- which he is fairly sure was bare when he passed
it, and elects not to be sure about --- sits a hammer. Old, good, the
handle worn to the colour of caramel. It fits his palm as though it has
been waiting to.

\"Well,\" Stepan tells the empty hall, \"I am not going to sleep next to
that.\"

He sets the head of the nail, braces the bowed casing flat with the heel
of his hand, and taps it home --- two light strokes and a third to seat
it, the wood drawing snug against the frame with a small, satisfied
tick. It is the most competent he has felt all day.

And as the nail goes home, something goes through him.

It is nothing --- less than nothing. A faint warm nudge somewhere behind
his breastbone, a shiver gone before he can name it, the sort of thing a
body does when a long day finally sets down its bags. \"Static,\" he
murmurs, by reflex, because it is becoming his word for the
inexplicable, and he rubs the heel of his hand and thinks no more about
it.

He does not hear the other sound. It is too small and too far, and his
ears are sixty-eight years old: downstairs, in the dark beneath the
counter, the brief dry scratch of a pen nib moving across paper, a few
unhurried words set down in a hand he has never seen. The sound is not
for him. It is a record being kept.

But the cat hears it.

It has trailed him down the landing at a distance, and now it goes still
in the particular way of cats, one ear rotating toward the stairs, its
amber eyes narrowing on the dark at the bottom as though it could read,
from here, the line that has just been written. Then it looks back at
Stepan with what he would swear, if he were paying attention, is
reappraisal --- a creature quietly adjusting a number it has been
keeping in its head.

Stepan is not paying attention. Stepan is done.

The day lands on him all at once, the way it does at his age: the train,
the road, the warm key, the impossible coffee, the bags that climbed the
stairs without him, the room laid out like an apology. He stands in the
bedroom doorway and tries, dutifully, to be troubled by all of it --- to
line the strangenesses up and make them account for themselves. The
coffee. The bags. The easy stairs. There is a thread here, his tired
mind insists, if he would only pull it ---

--- and then the bed is there, turned down and waiting, and the thread

He does not so much undress as concede. Coat over the chair, shoes
abandoned where they fall, and into the soft grey sweats and the
washed-thin t-shirt he has slept in for years, the closest thing he owns
to a friend. He means to lie down for a considering sort of moment. He
is asleep before the considering begins, face half into the pillow, one
hand still open as though it set something down and forgot to close.

For a while the room simply holds him.

Then, without a sound, the comforter at the foot of the bed begins to
move. Not flung, not pulled --- drawn, slow as a tide, creeping up over
his socked feet, his knees, settling along the curve of his back and up
to his shoulder, and tucking itself at the last against the back of his
neck the way a thing is tucked by someone who has done it many times and
was never once thanked.

The cat springs up onto the bed without disturbing it. It picks its way
to the pillow, stands over the sleeping man, then lowers its head and
puts its face close to his ear and stares in --- fixed, unblinking,
searching --- as though the answers it has been hunting all day, the
ones it cannot ask for and he cannot give, might after all be written
somewhere just inside, if it only looks long enough, and close enough,
and does not let itself hope.

Downstairs, the pen has stopped. The house breathes its cool mineral
breath. And Stepan Novacek sleeps in his uncle\'s shop on his first
night as its keeper, having understood almost nothing --- and having,
without the faintest notion that he has done it, made his very first
small dent in the dust.
