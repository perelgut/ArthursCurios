Inventory of Impossible Things
==============================

Chapter Seven: The Door-Breaker
-------------------------------

He has been standing in the dark for the better part of an hour, an old man with a stick, waiting for a war.

The amber line still hangs where he left it the night the scout came – *Magic Missile (I)* – a single rune of light a hand's width from his nose, casting no glow on the floor, patient as a price tag on nothing. He has not cleared it. He has instead, across two cold nights of waiting, done the other thing too many times to admit to a living soul: said the word just to watch the panel bloom out of the air, read it through, sent it away, and felt, each time, the small disgraceful thrill of a man with a new toy he cannot stop turning over in his hands.

"Status," he murmurs now, and there it is again – the tidy amber inventory of everything he is, his appalling Constitution included – and he looks at it the way other men his age look at their phones, for no reason, for company.

Then the cold comes, and he forgets he ever found it amusing.

It rolls out of the front room ahead of any sound, a flat mineral cold with green rot riding under it, and it is stronger than the scout's cold, stronger than anything yet. The baseboard gap – a hand's breadth two nights ago – gives with a wet tearing groan, the grey rotten wood folding back like a lip, and the dark inside it is the dark with distance in it, the dark that goes somewhere. Something fills that somewhere. Something that has been waiting for the door to be wide enough.

"Clear," he says, on reflex, and the *Status* panel vanishes, and he is alone in the dark with the cane and the one bright rune and whatever is about to arrive.

It arrives.

\* \* \*

This is not the small one.

The first goblin had been the size of a large cat, all elbows and appetite. This thing hauls itself through the widened gap a piece at a time, and when it unfolds to its full height in the grey light it is the size of a stooped child built out of knotted rope and slabs of grey-green muscle, its hands too big for it, its mouth a great wet overcrowded mess of teeth. It has no weapon. It does not need one. It smells of the gap, of thunderstorm and tidal rot, and when its flat black eyes find him they do not widen and they do not blink. They simply decide.

Stepan's whole body wants to climb something. There is nothing to climb.

He has the cane up before he has chosen to lift it, the brass ferrule pointed at the thing's chest, and the bright rune hangs in the corner of his eye like a promise he does not believe in. Because he has read the words. He knows what the book named the spell, and he knows what a magic missile is supposed to be in the stories, and he is also a practical man who has felt exactly one real thing come out of this cane in his life: a *zap*, a blue-white spark, enough to insult a tomcat. He is about to throw a parlour trick at a nightmare. He is sure, with the cold certainty of the about-to-die, that it will do nothing but make it angry.

The goblin gathers itself to spring.

He points the cane and he *means* it – not a word, not a gesture, only the cold flat want of *stop, stop there, no further* poured down the length of the ash and out through the brass – and the cane gives it a direction, and the want becomes light.

There is no spark. There is a sound like the world clearing its throat, and a line of force leaves the ferrule so fast it is already over, and the goblin does not shriek, does not stagger, does not get the chance. It simply ceases to be a thing that was about to spring and becomes a thing on the floor, flung the whole width of the front room, into the base of a shelf that cracks and rains its century of clutter down on top of it. The smell of scorched air. A single overturned snow-globe rolling in a slow arc across the boards.

Stepan stands with the cane still up and his arm ringing and his mouth open.

In the corner of his eye the amber writes one line and holds it, and he reads it three times before it means anything:

> *Magic Missile (I) – discharged.*

"That," he says, to the wrecked and silent room, "is not static."

\* \* \*

The fear comes a half-second late, the way it always does for him, and it comes in the shape of *more*. There is a hole in his wall that goes somewhere terrible and the somewhere is not empty, and a thing just came through it the size of a child, and there is no reason on earth there is only one.

So he does the thing his body has learned: he points the cane at the gaping dark and he reaches, again, for that flat cold *want.*

Nothing answers.

The cane is a stick. The brass is just brass. Whatever he poured down it is gone, scraped out of him, and where the bright rune was there is a different amber line now, and it does not reassure him:

> *Magic Missile (I) – recovering.*

No number. No little bar creeping toward full. No promise of when. Just the quiet bureaucratic word, *recovering*, in a hand that has never once told him a thing it did not feel like telling. He is sixty-eight years old, he has emptied himself doing something he does not understand, and he is standing in front of an open door to a hostile elsewhere armed with a length of ash and the dregs of his nerve.

"Right," he says, unsteadily. "Right."

*Breathe,* says Prince, into the middle of his skull, and he nearly comes out of his skin. The cat is on the counter, where it has been the whole time, tail wrapped neat over its feet, watching the dark with its ridiculous amber eyes. *Nothing else is coming tonight. It sent the big one because the big one was meant to widen the road. It did not expect the road to bite back quite so hard.* A pause, and something almost like approval, quickly hidden. *Neither, frankly, did I.*

"Is that – " He has to stop and start again. "Is that a compliment."

*It is an observation. Do not let it go to your head; there is very little room in there as it is.*

\* \* \*

The thing on the floor is not moving and will not move again, and Stepan makes himself go and look at it, because he is a man who looks at what he has done.

It is worse up close. It is worse because of the hands. Up close they are almost articulate, long-fingered, callused, the hands of something that uses tools and means harm, and the wrongness of having ended a thing with hands sits in him exactly where it sat after the first one, except heavier, because this one he killed at the length of a room without ever touching it, without effort, like swatting a fly the size of a person. He had wanted it to be hard. It being easy is its own kind of awful.

*The gap,* says Prince, and there is no mockery in it now. *Look at the gap.*

He looks. With the big one through, the breach has not closed behind it. It hangs open, a ragged grey wound in the baseboard wide enough now to put an arm through, and the cold still pours from it steady and patient, and far back in the wrong dark there is, he is almost certain, the suggestion of movement that has not yet decided to come.

"We have to close it."

*You do.* Prince drops from the counter and crosses to the gap, and does a thing it has never done: it stops short, and its fur goes up, and it will not put itself between the guardian and that dark. *I cannot shift the carcass. It is three times my weight and I have, you will have noticed, no hands worth the name.* The voice tightens. *You will not like the next part.*

He does not. The next part is that Prince, in clipped unwilling sentences, tells him to take the dead thing by its terrible articulate hands and feed it back into the hole it came from – to *plug* the gap with it, body first, folded into the wound in his wall like a cork into a bottle. To make of the corpse a stopper, and a message.

"You want me to – "

*I want the door shut. The body shuts it, for now, and it tells the next one what waits on this side.* A beat. *I did not say it would be pleasant. I said it would work.*

So he does it. He sets down the cane and he takes the cooling thing by its wrists, and it is heavy and it is wrong in every way a thing can be wrong to the hands, and he is, briefly and thoroughly, an old man being sick in the corner of his uncle's shop. And then he wipes his mouth and he goes back and he finishes, because it has to be finished, and he works the ruined body into the cold grey gap until the dark is stopped with it and the worst of the wrongness is, suddenly, behind a wall instead of in the room. The cold drops by half. The smell fades.

And the strange thing, the thing he does not expect: it is better. Not good. But the room, with the hole stopped, lets out a breath he did not know it had been holding, and so does he, and for one moment the horror and the guilt and the *yuck* of it all are answered, a little, by the plain animal relief of a door that is shut.

"That's not finished," he says, when he can speak. "Is it."

*No.* Prince has come to sit, pointedly, a little to the left – on a stretch of baseboard it has been guarding since the first day he held a hammer in this place. *It is held. It is not healed.* The cat looks at the rotten board beneath it, and then at him, and for a long moment it seems to be deciding how much it is allowed to say. *The house knew how to keep that door shut, once. Before it was let go to rot. The knowing is still here. It is in the – * and it stops, mid-thought, with an almost physical effort, and finishes, stiffly: *that is not entirely mine to tell. But you have been stepping over the answer all week, guardian. You mended everything in this building but the one thing that was holding it together.*

He follows the cat's eyes down to the board it will not move off of. The old carved baseboard, rotted soft, that he had filed under *mouse,* under *list,* under *later.*

"Oh," he says softly. "That."

\* \* \*

After that it is, of all things, carpentry.

He fetches the good tools and the offcut of seasoned board he has been saving, and he kneels – easily; the hip has long since stopped filing complaints worth the name – and he gets to work in the cold front room over a wall plugged with a monster, and it is the calmest he has felt in two days. This he understands. This he has done ten thousand times. He pries the soft rotten length free, and underneath it the wood is carved – he had never looked, never once looked, a line of interlocking shapes worn nearly to nothing along the board's hidden face, the same restless pattern that will not quite hold still on the cover of the book. Not decoration. He knows that now. He copies it as best an old man with a steady hand and a sharp chisel can copy a thing he does not understand, and he beds the new board true, and he drives it home.

He feels it seat. Not in his hands – under them, under the floor, a deep settling *click* like a bone going back where it belongs. The cold stops. Not retreats: stops, the way a draught stops when a door is finally hung square. The grey wound is a plain mended baseboard now, ordinary as anything in Ontario, and behind it the dark is still there and will always be there, but it is held, the way a good wall holds weather, the way it is supposed to be held.

He kneels there a moment with his hand flat on the new board, breathing, not thinking of much.

And under the quiet, almost too faint to be sure of, he hears it: a thin dry scratch, a nib travelling across a page, somewhere behind him on the counter, writing something down.

\* \* \*

The book is open when he gets to it, though he does not remember leaving it so, and the new ink is still wet enough to catch the light.

> *One intruder, larger. Dealt with at the length of a room. (He has found the reach of the gift. He has not yet found what it costs him; he will.)*
>
> *The breach plugged, and then – at last – the board reseated, true. (Adequate. The ward will hold a while. They always hold a while.)*

He reads it twice, the dry approval and the dry warning folded into one another the way the book likes, and he finds he is too tired to be unsettled and too unsettled to be proud, and lands somewhere in between, which is roughly where the book seems to want him.

He becomes aware, as he sits, that the room is quietly putting itself back together. The shelf the goblin cracked when it died – he had forgotten it, in everything that came after – is knitting whole again, the splintered upright drawing straight, the century of spilled clutter rising off the boards and resettling into its places. And the one thing that had broken in the fall, a small painted snow-globe, lifts itself from the floor and stands again on the mended shelf, its glass uncracked, its little blizzard slowing to a gentle drift. The shop is healing the damage of its own defence. *That,* he thinks, *is what the work buys* – not warm keys and good coffee only, but this: a building that mends itself, because he tends it.

Then the air says *blurp.*

It is not a word he has ever needed before to describe a sound in a place of business, and yet it is the only one that fits: a small, wet, cheerful *blurp,* from the corner by the monolith, and he turns with the cane half-up out of pure trained dread – and stops.

There is a machine there that was not there an hour ago. It is squat and round and faintly absurd, churning to itself with quiet industry, and it is, unmistakably, an ice-cream maker. As he watches, it gives another contented *blurp* and settles, keeping its bowl, he somehow knows, at exactly the right temperature, against exactly no demand that anyone has made.

He looks at it. He looks at the cat.

"Tonight," he says. "It decided tonight was the night for that."

*It directs its own growth,* says Prince, with the weary patience of a creature explaining gravity to a stone. *It does not ask permission, and it has a sense of occasion that I have long since stopped trying to account for.* A pause. *It is, I will grant you, very good ice-cream.*

And so it is that an old man who has, in one night, killed a monster at the length of a room, corked a hole in the world with its body, re-warded his dead uncle's shop with a chisel, and emptied himself of a power he is only beginning to believe in – ends that night sitting on the tall stool at the counter at two in the morning, eating, with grave attention, a small bowl of genuinely excellent vanilla, while a cat that is not a cat watches him and says nothing, and the book lies closed and satisfied under his other hand.

His uncle went out one night, he thinks, into the dark, and never came back, and everyone called it a fantastical journey. He looks at the mended board, and the dark held behind it, and the door he has just learned how to shut, and he thinks: *you went through one of these, didn't you. You found one that wouldn't hold, and you went through.* The thought should frighten him. Mostly it makes him want, absurdly and for the first time, to find the old man and have a word.

He finishes the ice-cream. He rinses the bowl. And then, because he is who he is, and because the toy is still new and the night has earned it, he says it once more to the empty amber air, quietly, just to see his own particulars hanging there safe and accounted for:

"Status."

The panel unrolls, patient and gold. *Magic Missile (I) – recovering,* it still says, in its corner, honest to the last.

"Yes," he tells it. "You and me both."

"Clear," he says – and goes, at last, to bed.
